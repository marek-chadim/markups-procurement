\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Firms participating in Czech public procurement charge markups approximately 14\% higher than non-participants, with heterogeneity across NACE sub-industries (17\% in buildings, 6\% in civil engineering, 11\% in specialized trades). This premium is robust across nine estimation methods and 33 specifications---markup \emph{levels} range from 1.02 to 2.17 depending on functional form and variable input choice, but the treatment effect is stable at 12.5--14.3\%---because it is identified from cross-firm cost-share variation (observed data), not from the estimated output elasticity (Gandhi, Navarro, and Rivers 2020). This is the first application of the Andrews, Barahona, Gentzkow, Rambachan, and Shapiro (2025) transparency framework to markup premium evaluation. The premium is point-identified through an external instrument---lagged procurement status in the productivity Markov transition, following De Loecker (2013)---which breaks the Gandhi, Navarro, and Rivers (2020) non-identification result for gross-output production functions. All ACF instruments satisfy ABGRS strong exclusion (partial $R^2 < 0.10$), so the premium achieves approximate causal consistency under local production-function misspecification. The Cobb-Douglas and translog specifications are treated as equivalently valid and yield the same treatment effect.

Using contract-level engineer estimates from the Czech tender register, I quantify the fiscal welfare cost of the premium: capping procurement contracts at their engineer estimates would save 10~bn~CZK per year (3.6\% of procurement spending), while pricing at marginal cost implies savings of 70~bn~CZK per year (12.3\%). The within-firm markup-to-price pass-through is 0.81, concentrated in NACE~42 civil engineering where the top 10\% of contracts account for 72\% of the implied fiscal transfer. The premium declined from 30\% in 2006 to 10\% in 2021, with the 2012 single-bidding ban and 2016 MEAT criteria reform each associated with significant reductions.

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Do rising markups reflect growing market power? De Loecker, Eeckhout, and Unger \cite{DeLoekerEeckhout2020} document that US markups rose from 21\% above marginal cost in 1980 to 61\% in 2016, concentrated among high-markup firms. Benkard, Miller, and Yurukoglu \cite{BMY2026} challenge this finding: the estimated trend is sensitive to production function specification choices---particularly the variable input definition and overhead cost treatment. In their reply, De Loecker, Eeckhout, and Unger \cite{DLEU2025reply} trace the divergence to outliers in a single four-digit industry and show that output elasticities are stable over time, with the trend driven entirely by expenditure shares. The exchange reveals a deeper question: if markup \emph{levels} are specification-sensitive, can markup-based \emph{treatment effects} be trusted? The setting also makes the underlying public-economics question concrete. In a competitive procurement market, winning bids should equal marginal cost; a nonzero procurement markup premium is therefore direct evidence that competition is ``missing'' from an institution whose formal rules are designed to produce it---the market-power diagnostic at the center of the modern public-economics toolkit.

This paper brings the production approach to a setting where that question can be answered directly. Czech construction procurement provides a binary treatment---procurement participation---against which to measure whether specification sensitivity in markup estimation propagates to causal estimates. Three findings emerge. First, firms participating in public procurement charge markups approximately 14\% higher than non-participants, with an event study showing flat pre-trends and a sharp jump at entry. Second, this premium is robust across nine distinct estimation methods: while markup \emph{levels} range from 1.02 to 2.17, the treatment effect is stable at 12.5--14.3\% across all methods except OLS. Third, the premium has declined from 30\% in 2006 to 10\% in 2021, tracking a sequence of Czech transparency reforms.

Czech construction is a clean testbed for the production approach. Cost of goods sold---dominated by cement, steel, concrete, and fuel---constitutes approximately 50\% of gross output, providing a well-measured variable input with stable cost shares. The industry has been studied extensively in the markup literature: Collard-Wexler and De Loecker \cite{CWDLKvsOmega} analyze US steel using the same production approach. Between 2006 and 2021, Czech policymakers enacted a series of transparency reforms---the 2012 single-bidding ban, the 2016 shift to MEAT criteria, and the 2017 Register of Contracts---providing time-series variation in the scope for discretion and favoritism.

The estimation follows De Loecker and Warzynski \cite{DLW2012}: the markup equals the ratio of the output elasticity of a variable input to its expenditure share. Output elasticities are recovered from a translog production function estimated via Ackerberg, Caves, and Frazer \cite{ACF2015}, with the productivity law of motion conditioned on lagged procurement status $pp_{it-1}$ following De Loecker \cite{DeLoecker2013}. The lagged procurement indicator is an \emph{external} shifter---predetermined with respect to current-period input choices---that breaks the Gandhi, Navarro, and Rivers \cite{GNR2020} non-identification result for gross-output production functions (see the detailed discussion in De Loecker and Syverson \cite{DLS2021}, Handbook of Industrial Organization Vol.~4, Ch.~3, \S5.3). Point identification is therefore restored in our setting, and the premium is identified via the external instrument, not projected onto the observed cost share. For the revenue-based dependent variable, the Quality Equivalent Cournot model of Ackerberg and De Loecker \cite{ADLQEC2026} justifies consistent estimation: within-market revenue differences reflect quality-adjusted quantities at a common price, so revenue is the preferred output measure for Czech construction where firm-specific unit definitions are not observed.

The paper makes two contributions. First, this is the first application of the Andrews, Barahona, Gentzkow, Rambachan, and Shapiro \cite{ABGRS2025} transparency framework to markup premium evaluation. The procurement premium is point-identified through an external instrument---lagged procurement status $pp_{it-1}$ in the productivity Markov transition, following De Loecker \cite{DeLoecker2013}---which breaks the Gandhi, Navarro, and Rivers \cite{GNR2020} non-identification result for gross-output production functions. ABGRS strong exclusion, the mean independence of moment-condition instruments from the control set, is then verified empirically: all ACF instruments satisfy partial $R^2 < 0.10$ (Section~\ref{sec:abgrs}), so the procurement premium achieves approximate causal consistency under local misspecification of the production function, even as markup \emph{levels} range from 1.02 to 2.17 across the nine estimators that Benkard, Miller, and Yurukoglu \cite{BMY2026} flag as specification-sensitive. The Cobb-Douglas and translog specifications are treated as equivalently valid throughout. Second, the fiscal welfare cost of the premium is quantifiable at the contract level. Using contract-level engineer estimates from the Czech tender register, I establish that the within-firm markup-to-price pass-through is $0.81$ and concentrated in NACE~42 civil engineering, where the top $10\%$ of contracts account for $72\%$ of the implied fiscal transfer (Section~\ref{sec:welfare}).

Before turning to the literature, I address the natural question---asked by both the applied-macro reader and the BLP-trained industrial-organization referee---of why the production-function detour is necessary when the reduced-form ratio of contract prices to engineer estimates (Baran\v{e}k and Titl \cite{BaranekTitl2024}) is already available. Three features of the markup lens are unavailable from a reduced-form price ratio. First, the markup isolates a firm's rent over marginal cost and thereby permits the productivity-selection decomposition that reconciles the $14\%$ markup premium with Baran\v{e}k and Titl's $6\%$ political-connection price premium; Section~\ref{sec:favoritism} Panel~F provides a direct Rel\_Price test showing that controlling for engineer estimates reduces the premium only from $0.149$ to $0.141$, i.e., the overpricing channel accounts for roughly $5\%$ of the premium, and the remainder is productivity selection. A price-over-engineer-estimate ratio confounds rent with the baseline industry markup already embedded in the engineer benchmark and therefore cannot support such a decomposition. Second, the ABGRS strong-exclusion diagnostic introduced above is approximately causally consistent under local misspecification of the production function---including the multiproduct concern of Pakes \cite{Pakes2021AnnRev} that revenue-based PF estimation does not recover structural output elasticities for firms producing across multiple project categories---because the treatment-effect differential loads on cross-firm variation in observed cost shares rather than on the estimated output elasticity. A reduced-form price ratio has no structural object to misspecify and therefore no machinery to defend itself against functional-form skepticism. Third, only the structural object admits a marginal-cost counterfactual: Section~\ref{sec:welfare} shows that capping bids at engineer estimates would save $3.6\%$ of procurement spending while pricing at marginal cost would save $12.3\%$, and the $8.66$ percentage-point gap between these two benchmarks is the structural policy space that a reduced-form analysis cannot reach.

The paper relates most closely to five benchmarks. Kroft, Luo, Mogstad, and Setzler \cite{KLMS2025} estimate buyer-side wage markdowns in US construction auctions; I isolate the seller-side markup premium using binary procurement-entry variation and fifteen years of Czech institutional change. Ackerberg and De Loecker \cite{ADL2024} derive sufficient-statistic corrections for imperfect-competition bias in production-function estimation; I show the correction is null for the treatment differential when the input technology is near-Leontief, because the correction enters symmetrically across treated and control firms. De Ridder, Grassi, and Morzenti \cite{DGM2026} compare estimation methods on simulated data; I show the nine-method convergence holds on real policy-relevant data because the premium is identified from observed cost shares, not from estimated elasticities. De Loecker and Scott \cite{DeLoeckerScott2025} validate production-approach markups against a Miller-Weinberg demand system for US beer, finding cross-method level agreement in the 2007 overlap year; I pursue within-production robustness because Czech procurement records winning bids only---full bid vectors are unavailable pre-2017 and have approximately 50\% coverage after---making a BLP demand system or structural auction triangulation infeasible in my setting. Baran\v{e}k and Titl \cite{BaranekTitl2024} estimate a 6\% political-connection premium in Czech procurement using electoral variation; the 14\% total markup premium I recover is consistent with their finding once the productivity-selection component (approximately 11 percentage points) is netted out, leaving a residual pricing-power component bounded above by the political-connection channel they identify.

\section{Setting and Data}\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Czech Construction}\label{sec:setting}

The Czech construction sector accounts for 49.6\% of all public procurement contracts, approximately 7\% of GDP, and employs over 7\% of the labor force. It is also among the least competitive procurement markets in the European Union: single-bidder tenders exceed 48\% of awarded contracts, and each additional bidder reduces prices by roughly 3.3\% \cite{PalgutaPertold2017}. The sector comprises three CZ-NACE sub-industries: NACE 41 (construction of buildings), NACE 42 (civil engineering), and NACE 43 (specialized construction activities).

These reforms provide exogenous time-series variation in competition intensity. Palguta and Pertold-Gebicka \cite{PalgutaPertold2017} document that below-threshold manipulation allowed politically connected firms to avoid competitive tendering before the reforms, while Baran\v{e}k and Titl \cite{BaranekTitl2024} show that political connections raise procurement prices by 6\%, with EU co-funding oversight fully mitigating this overpricing.

\subsection{Data Sources}\label{sec:datasources}

Three Czech data sources are combined, covering 2005--2023.

\textbf{Firm financials.} The MagnusWeb database provides annual financial statements for Czech firms classified in CZ-NACE division F (construction). Key variables include sales (GO), cost of goods sold (COGS), number of employees (L), and fixed assets (K). The raw dataset also contains wages (W) and intermediate inputs (II) separately at full coverage, enabling decomposition of GO $\approx$ W + II. All nominal variables are deflated using OECD industry deflators.

\textbf{Public procurement.} The Datlab register contains the universe of Czech public procurement contracts, compiled from the official ISVZ system. For each tender, the data record the contract value, signature date, procurement procedure, awarding entity, and the identities of all bidders. Procurement records are merged to firm financials using company identifiers. The primary treatment variable is $pp_{it}$, a binary indicator equal to one if firm $i$ has an active procurement contract in year $t$.

\textbf{Sample construction.} The estimation sample is rebuilt from raw CSVs using a transparent Python pipeline (\texttt{rebuild\_data.py}). Key steps include: (i) 1:1 merges with deduplication, (ii) tender year assigned from signature date, (iii) winsorization at the 2nd and 98th percentiles by NACE 2-digit on log variables following De Ridder, Grassi, and Morzenti \cite{DGM2026}, and (iv) trimming observations with overhead-to-gross-output ratios exceeding 0.90. The resulting sample contains 1,521 firms and 9,164 firm-year observations.

\textbf{Orbis financial statements.} To overcome MagnusWeb's limitation of categorical employment brackets, I supplement with Bureau van Dijk's Orbis database accessed through WRDS. Orbis provides continuous employee headcounts, cost of employees, operating revenue, cost of goods sold, and fixed assets for Czech firms across all industries. Merging on company identifiers (I\v{C}O), 84\% of MagnusWeb construction firms appear in Orbis (1,260 of 1,498). Capital correlates at 0.983 and wages at 0.951 between the two sources.

Table \ref{tab:summary} reports summary statistics for the estimation sample. Panel~B compares treated and control firms: procurement participants are larger but operate at similar cost structures, consistent with selection on scale rather than efficiency.

\subsection{Data Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

Four limitations bear mention. First, the Datlab tender data include only winning bid prices, not losing bids, precluding estimation of a full demand system or structural auction model in the tradition of Hendricks and Porter \cite{HendricksPorter1988}, Haile and Tamer \cite{HaileTamer2003}, and Athey and Haile \cite{AtheyHaile2007}---the natural alternative framework, exemplified by Compiani, Haile, and Sant'Anna \cite{CompianiHaileSantAnna2020} for U.S.\ offshore lease auctions---and restricting the analysis to the production approach of De Loecker and Warzynski \cite{DLW2012}. Second, MagnusWeb employment is reported in categorical brackets rather than continuous counts, limiting labor supply elasticity estimation. Third, the tender data do not flag EU co-funding, precluding a direct test of the intergovernmental oversight channel identified by Baran\'{e}k and Titl \cite{BaranekTitl2024}. Fourth, output is measured as deflated revenues, not physical quantities. Foster, Haltiwanger, and Syverson \cite{FHS2008} show that revenue-based TFP confounds physical productivity with pricing power; the DLW markup formula partially addresses this by extracting the markup component, but residual price variation within deflator categories remains a source of measurement error. De Ridder, Grassi, and Morzenti \cite{DGM2026} show that this bias is modest for cross-firm markup \emph{variation} and treatment effects, even when markup \emph{levels} are affected---the procurement premium as a ratio is therefore robust.

\section{Production Approach to Markup Estimation}\label{sec:methodology}

Production function estimation serves a purpose beyond TFP measurement: it recovers the cost structure needed to assess allocative efficiency. Market power causes production to shift toward higher-cost firms, generating welfare losses distinct from the standard markup triangle \cite{AskerCWDL2019}. In the procurement context, this question is first-order: does the markup premium reflect efficient selection of low-cost producers into government contracting, or misallocation of contracts to firms with pricing power?

\subsection{Markup Formula}\label{sec:markup_formula}

Under cost minimization, a firm equates the output elasticity of each freely adjustable input to the product of its markup and the input's expenditure share. This yields the markup formula of De Loecker and Warzynski \cite{DLW2012}, building on Hall \cite{Hall1988}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:markup}
    \mu_{it} = \frac{\theta^V_{it}}{\alpha^V_{it}},
\end{equation}
where $\theta^V_{it} = \partial \ln Y_{it} / \partial \ln V_{it}$ is the output elasticity of the variable input and $\alpha^V_{it} = P^V_{it} V_{it} / (P_{it} Y_{it})$ is the expenditure share of the variable input in total revenue. Under perfect competition $\mu_{it} = 1$ and $\theta^V_{it} = \alpha^V_{it}$; deviations from unity indicate market power. Appendix~\ref{app:markup_derivation} provides the full derivation from profit maximization.

The formula rests on three assumptions: (i) firms minimize variable cost conditional on output and fixed inputs; (ii) productivity is Hicks-neutral; and (iii) productivity evolves as a first-order Markov process conditional on lagged treatment status. The third assumption, established by De Loecker \cite{DeLoecker2013} for learning-by-exporting and extended by De Loecker and Syverson \cite{DLS2021} to general treatment evaluation, ensures internal consistency whenever the treatment may affect productivity dynamics. Following the identification framework of Berry and Haile \cite{BerryHaile2018}, these three assumptions jointly define the admissible set of structures within which the firm-year markup $\mu_{it}$ is the structural feature of interest; the task of the estimation section is to show that $\mu_{it}$ is uniquely determined within this set by the joint distribution of $(Y, V, K, pp)$.

An alternative to this production-side approach is the demand-side framework for differentiated products, reviewed by Berry and Haile \cite{BerryHaile2018}, which recovers markups from estimated demand elasticities. Three features of the procurement setting make that framework infeasible here. First, each construction contract is a one-shot project-specific event, not a repeated choice across differentiated products. Second, the unit of observation is the contract, not a market share---there is no natural analog to the share-inversion step at the heart of BLP identification. Third, no instruments are available to generate exogenous variation in demand faced by a given firm across tenders. Conlon, Miller, Otgon, and Yao \cite{CMOY2023}---our closest methodological kin---circumvent the first two obstacles using exclusion restrictions on cost shifters, but their setting relies on aggregated product-level data that procurement lacks.

\subsection{Estimation}\label{sec:estimation}

Estimating the output elasticity $\theta^V_{it}$ from production data confronts a fundamental simultaneity problem: firms observe their productivity $\omega_{it}$ and adjust variable inputs accordingly, biasing OLS upward for the variable input coefficient. Fixed effects do not solve this---they require the implausible assumption that productivity is time-invariant ($\omega_{it} = \omega_i$), and first-differencing exacerbates measurement error in capital, compressing $\hat{\beta}_k$ toward zero. Olley and Pakes \cite{OlleyPakes1996} propose a structural solution: invert a monotone policy function (investment) to recover $\omega_{it} = f_t^{-1}(k_{it}, i_{it})$, then use the Markov property of productivity to form moment conditions that identify the capital coefficient. Ackerberg, Caves, and Frazer \cite{ACF2015} refine this approach, replacing investment with the variable input demand function as the proxy and resolving a collinearity problem in the first stage. In construction, materials procurement (cement, steel, concrete orders) precedes labor hiring for each project, satisfying the ACF timing assumption that the proxy variable is chosen before labor adjusts to contemporaneous productivity shocks. The Cobb-Douglas specification is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cd}
    y_{it} = \beta_0 + \beta_k k_{it} + \beta_{\text{cogs}} \text{cogs}_{it} + \omega_{it} + \varepsilon_{it},
\end{equation}
and the translog specification:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tl}
    y_{it} = \beta_0 + \beta_k k_{it} + \beta_v v_{it} + \beta_{kk} k_{it}^2 + \beta_{vv} v_{it}^2 + \beta_{kv} k_{it} v_{it} + \omega_{it} + \varepsilon_{it},
\end{equation}
where $y_{it}$ is log deflated sales, $k_{it}$ is log capital, and $v_{it}$ is log variable input.

Estimation proceeds in two stages. The first stage recovers $\hat{\phi}_{it}$ from a flexible polynomial in inputs and state variables. The second stage forms moment conditions $\E[\xi_{it}(\beta) \cdot \mathbf{Z}_{it}] = 0$, where $\xi_{it} = \omega_{it} - g(\omega_{it-1}, pp_{it-1})$ is the innovation to the productivity Markov process and $\mathbf{Z}_{it} = (1, k_{it}, \text{cogs}_{it-1})$. The Markov transition includes the lagged procurement indicator:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\omega}_{it} = \rho \hat{\omega}_{it-1} + \gamma pp_{it-1} + \delta \hat{p}_{it-1} + \xi_{it},
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p}_{it-1}$ is the predicted survival probability from a probit model, addressing the Olley-Pakes selection problem: surviving firms have systematically higher productivity innovations ($E[\xi_{it} | k_{it}, \text{active at } t] \neq 0$), biasing the Markov regression unless one conditions on the exit threshold \cite{OlleyPakes1996}. Including $pp_{it-1}$ in the law of motion is not a robustness check---it is the only specification consistent with the model under any hypothesis where procurement can affect productivity through learning-by-doing, scale economies, or technology transfer \cite{DeLoecker2013,DLS2021}. The control function inversion requires a scalar unobservable assumption: $\omega_{it}$ must be the only serially correlated component that the firm observes when choosing inputs. This rules out unobserved cross-firm input price variation and measurement error in the proxy variable---assumptions that are more plausible for materials (COGS) than for labor \cite{DGM2026}. Analytical standard errors follow the GMM sandwich formula of Ackerberg, Chen, and Hahn \cite{ACH2012}, with firm-clustered meat matrix and delta method for markup SEs.\footnote{The second-stage GMM is a minimum-distance estimator in the sense of Chamberlain \cite{Chamberlain1984}, \S4.2. Our use of $k_{t-1}$ and $\text{cogs}_{t-2}$ as predetermined instruments corresponds to the sequential moment restriction framework of Chamberlain \cite{Chamberlain2022}, which relaxes strict exogeneity to allow feedback from lagged outcomes to current inputs---the relevant case in construction, where a firm that wins a contract at $t{-}1$ plausibly adjusts input choices at $t$. The ACF proxy-variable approach is a nonparametric control-function identification strategy (Imbens and Newey \cite{ImbensNewey2009}; Matzkin \cite{Matzkin2003}); Chen and Pouzo \cite{ChenPouzo2012,ChenPouzo2015} develop the sieve minimum-distance theory for the conditional moment restrictions used in the second-stage GMM.}

\subsection{Variable Input Choice}\label{sec:variable_input}

The choice of variable input is the most consequential specification decision. Cost of goods sold (COGS) constitutes approximately 50\% of gross output in Czech construction, dominated by cement, steel, concrete, and fuel---materials plausibly under short-run adjustment. Intermediate inputs (II), at approximately 83\% of gross output, compress the expenditure share toward the output elasticity, mechanically pushing markups toward unity.

Raval \cite{Raval2023} formalizes the concern: under cost minimization with Hicks-neutral productivity, \emph{any} flexible input should recover the same markup. His finding that labor and materials markups are negatively correlated across seven countries rejects this overidentifying restriction. In the Czech construction panel, labor-based and COGS-based markups are positively correlated ($\hat{\beta} = 0.28$--$0.82$), preserving firm rankings even though $\beta = 1$ is rejected. The positive Czech correlation means the treatment effect is preserved even when overidentification fails.

De Ridder, Grassi, and Morzenti \cite{DGM2026} provide a further justification: if the variable input faces a monopsony wedge, the production approach measures the product of the markup and the input wedge, making COGS the cleaner measure. The sensitivity analysis in Section~\ref{sec:nine_methods} confirms: using II instead of COGS flips the premium sign, while all other specification choices leave it intact.

\subsection{Specification Sensitivity}\label{sec:sensitivity}

The central methodological finding is that markup \emph{levels} are specification-sensitive but the procurement \emph{premium} is not. The sensitivity has a clean identification explanation. Gandhi, Navarro, and Rivers \cite{GNR2020} show that gross-output production functions are generically non-identified from internal instruments alone (lagged inputs), but this non-identification is \emph{broken} whenever the Markov law of motion for productivity conditions on an external shifter predetermined with respect to current-period input choices. Following De Loecker \cite{DeLoecker2013} and the detailed discussion in De Loecker and Syverson \cite{DLS2021} (Handbook of Industrial Organization, Vol.~4, Ch.~3, \S5.3), I include lagged procurement status $pp_{it-1}$ in the Markov transition. This external shifter restores point identification of the production function coefficients: the premium is \emph{point-identified} via the external instrument, not cost-share projected. The stability of the premium across estimators (ACF, OLS, Blundell-Bond \cite{BlundellBond1998}) and functional forms (Cobb-Douglas, translog) is therefore convergent point-identification evidence, not identification failure. For revenue-based output---the measure available in the Czech MagnusWeb panel---Ackerberg and De Loecker's \cite{ADLQEC2026} Quality Equivalent Cournot model justifies revenue as the preferred production-function dependent variable: within-market revenue variation is at a constant price, so revenue differences across firms reflect only quality-adjusted quantities, yielding consistent production-function coefficients even when firms' unit definitions differ across construction contracts. The premium is specification-sensitive only when an alternative variable input changes the expenditure share on which the markup formula is evaluated---confirming that the variable input definition, not the estimator, is the binding specification decision.

De Loecker, Eeckhout, and Unger \cite{DLEU2025reply} make the same point for aggregate trends: output elasticities are stable, and all variation flows through the expenditure share. The Conlon, Miller, Otgon, and Yao \cite{CMOY2023} finding that markup growth is uncorrelated with PPI changes ($R^2 < 0.001$) underscores that cross-sectional cost-share variation, rather than production function estimation, drives markup measurement.

\textbf{Estimand under misspecification.} Andrews, Barahona, Gentzkow, Rambachan, and Shapiro \cite{ABGRS2025} formalize when structural estimates remain reliable despite model misspecification: the estimator achieves ``approximate causal consistency'' if the instruments satisfy \emph{strong exclusion}---mean independence from the included controls. Under strong exclusion, the procurement premium remains approximately correct even if the translog production function is wrong. This is also the empirical response to Pakes's \cite{Pakes2021AnnRev} concern (\S3.1, p.~411) that revenue-based production-function estimation does not recover a structural output elasticity for \emph{multiproduct} firms, because the aggregate input allocation across products depends on demand parameters. Czech construction firms are typically multiproduct---bidding simultaneously across NACE~41 buildings, NACE~42 civil engineering, and NACE~43 specialized trades, and across distinct project categories within each---so the Pakes critique is binding on markup \emph{levels}. It washes out of the procurement treatment-effect \emph{differential} under strong exclusion, because the differential loads on cross-firm variation in the observed cost share $\alpha^V_{it}$ rather than on the estimated elasticity $\hat\theta^V_{it}$---the same mechanism that makes the premium stable at 12.5--14.3\% while levels vary from 1.02 to 2.17.\footnote{Dhyne, Petrin, Smeets, and Warzynski \cite{DhynePetrinSmeetsWarzynski2020} develop the theoretical extension of the DLW markup formula to multiproduct settings that Pakes \cite{Pakes2021AnnRev} points to as the methodological path forward. Their approach requires firm-product-level disaggregated output and input data, which the Czech MagnusWeb panel does not provide (COGS is reported at the firm-year level only, not decomposed across NACE 41/42/43 project categories). The ABGRS strong-exclusion diagnostic applied here is therefore the feasible-data substitute, targeting the procurement treatment-effect differential rather than the firm-specific output elasticity that the multiproduct extension would recover.} If the true technology is Leontief---as Kroft, Luo, Mogstad, and Setzler \cite{KLMS2025} suggest for construction and as De Loecker and Scott \cite{DeLoeckerScott2025} adopt as their main specification for US brewing---the COGS elasticity collapses to a constant and the markup formula simplifies to the cost-share ratio, which the data observe directly. The null ADL imperfect-competition correction (Section~\ref{sec:adl}) is therefore an expected consequence of near-Leontief technology, not a failure of the IC framework. Section~\ref{sec:abgrs} verifies that all ACF instruments have partial $R^2 < 0.10$ on the control set, confirming strong exclusion on the Czech panel.

\textbf{Proxy-free alternative estimator.} A frontier alternative by Norris Keiller, de Paula, and Van Reenen \cite{NorrisKeillerdePaulaVanReenen2024} avoids the proxy-variable monotonicity assumption entirely by using firm-level probabilistic expectations of future output and inputs---available in recent UK Office for National Statistics surveys---to recover production function parameters without a parametric Markov for productivity. Their method is robust to optimization error and unobserved input-price variation that would compromise OP/LP/ACF, but requires firm-level subjective expectations data unavailable in the Czech setting. I therefore rely on the Ackerberg-Caves-Frazer estimator with ABGRS strong-exclusion diagnostics as the feasible-data alternative to their ideal-data estimator. A related caveat concerns the expenditure share $\alpha^V_{it}$ that enters the markup formula itself: the canonical markup definition $\mu_{it} = \theta^V_{it}/\alpha^V_{it}$ presumes that firms' COGS choices satisfy frictionless cost minimization. If Czech construction firms systematically deviate---through bulk cement discounts, VAT-timing inventory adjustments, or working capital constraints at bid-cycle boundaries---then $\alpha^V_{it}$ is a noisy proxy for the true flexible input share, and the measured markup inherits that noise. The Norris Keiller et al.\ estimator is robust to precisely this failure mode; our ABGRS strong-exclusion defense, paired with the cost-share correlation evidence of Section~\ref{sec:variable_input}, is the feasible-data substitute.

The Markov specification is the second key choice. Including the procurement dummy triples the regression-adjusted premium (from 0.003 to 0.138), because the plain specification attributes all procurement-induced productivity gains to the residual, biasing the markup premium toward zero. De Loecker and Syverson \cite{DLS2021} make this point with particular force: ``if the changes in the environment are part of the information set of a producer, they should be included in the model for forecasting productivity'' (p.~196).

\subsection{Production Function Estimates}\label{sec:pf_coefs}

Table \ref{tab:pf_estimates} reports the estimated production function coefficients in the format of De Loecker and Scott \cite{DeLoeckerScott2025} (Table C2): two panels for Cobb-Douglas and translog, each spanning a pooled column with year $\times$ NACE 2-digit fixed effects and three by-industry columns. The pooled column pins down a single industry-invariant scalar; the by-industry columns allow heterogeneous output elasticities across NACE 41 (buildings), 42 (civil engineering), and 43 (specialized construction). Panel B adds Kim, Luo, and Su (2019) deeper lags $(k_{t-1}, \text{cogs}_{t-2})$ as excluded instruments, providing two degrees of overidentification that enable the Hansen $J$ test and regularize the higher-order translog terms.

Within each industry, the Cobb-Douglas and translog specifications yield nearly identical average markups (Panel A vs.\ Panel B $\bar\mu$: NACE 41 $\approx$ 2.14, NACE 42 $\approx$ 2.35, NACE 43 $\approx$ 1.70), confirming the De Ridder, Grassi, and Morzenti \cite{DGM2026} result that functional-form choice moves markup \emph{levels} negligibly once cost-share information is exploited. The translog is the preferred specification because the firm-year output elasticity $\theta^V_{it} = \hat\beta_c + 2\hat\beta_{cc} c_{it} + \hat\beta_{kc} k_{it}$ varies across the input-bundle distribution, admitting heterogeneous markups that the Cobb-Douglas cannot represent. The translog linear $\hat\beta_c$ coefficient (1.372 for NACE 41, 1.087 for NACE 42, 0.851 for NACE 43) is not an output elasticity itself but the intercept of that firm-specific elasticity function. Standard errors on the NACE 43 translog linear term (0.997) are an order of magnitude larger than the corresponding NACE 41/42 values, reflecting the analytic sandwich variance inflation when the mean input bundle sits close to the boundary of the region where $\theta^V_{it}$ is identified; the point estimates themselves match the Cobb-Douglas $\bar\mu$ to within 0.03. The cross-terms ($\hat\beta_{k^2}$, $\hat\beta_{c^2}$, $\hat\beta_{kc}$) are individually small but jointly large enough to produce meaningful heterogeneity in $\theta^V_{it}$.

\subsection{Markup Distributions}\label{sec:markups}

Average markups differ substantially across sub-industries: NACE 41 (buildings) averages 2.14, NACE 42 (civil engineering) 2.35, and NACE 43 (specialized construction) 1.71. These levels---prices 70--110\% above variable cost---may appear large for a competitive industry with 3--8\% profit margins. However, markups measure price relative to \emph{variable} cost only; fixed costs (land, equipment, overhead) drive the wedge. As Hall~\cite{Hall2018} emphasizes, an industry with 5\% profit margins can sustain markups of 2.0 if fixed costs account for 47\% of revenue. The Cobb-Douglas specification (Spec E) yields nearly identical means (2.16, 2.34, 1.71), confirming that the functional form choice moves levels negligibly. The levels are higher than in an earlier version of this paper based on a smaller sample (1,297 vs.\ 1,521 firms) because the rebuilt dataset corrects for survivorship-biased firm selection and uses industry-specific deflators that reduce downward bias in real output.

The unconditional log markup difference between procurement and non-procurement firms is 0.180 (SE $= 0.004$). Controlling for year$\times$NACE fixed effects and input levels, the regression-adjusted premium is $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.138$ (SE $= 0.002$), implying a 14.8\% procurement markup premium. Czech construction markups fall within the range documented in the broader European literature \cite{DiezLeighTambunlertchai2018}.

\paragraph{What $\hat{\beta}_1$ actually measures.} The interpretation follows directly from De Loecker and Warzynski \cite{DLW2012}~(\S III, p.~2458) for the export-vs-domestic case that is the canonical analog of procurement-vs-private in construction: the ACF markup $\hat{\mu}_{it} = \hat{\theta}^V_{it}/\hat{\alpha}^V_{it}$ is ``a share-weighted average markup across the two markets, where the weight by market is the share of an input's expenditure used in production sold in that market.'' Czech construction firms are rarely pure-procurement or pure-private---the average procurement-active firm derives roughly $42\%$ of its revenue from government contracts---so $\hat{\beta}_1 = 0.138$ measures the average effect of having \emph{any} procurement presence on this share-weighted firm-year markup, not the per-contract markup differential between procurement and private contracts. As DLW note, contract-level identification of the within-firm markup differential would require disaggregated input-allocation data by final market or additional theoretical structure, neither of which is available in Czech construction: COGS is reported at the firm-year level, not decomposed across the firm's procurement and private project portfolios. DLW \cite{DLW2012}~(p.~2458) correspondingly treat their export-dummy coefficient $\hat{\delta}_1$ as descriptive---``we are not interpreting $\delta_1$ as a causal parameter''---and this paper departs from that stance by providing four independent causal diagnostics for $\hat{\beta}_1$: ABGRS strong exclusion (Section~\ref{sec:abgrs}), the near-symmetric entry/exit reversal of Section~\ref{sec:entry_exit}, flat pre-trends in the event study of Section~\ref{sec:event_study}, and the Oster bound $\delta^* = -7.3$ of Section~\ref{sec:favoritism}. Two features of the downstream analysis reconcile the share-weighted firm-year coefficient with contract-level welfare. First, the continuous intensive-margin dose-response of Section~\ref{sec:productivity_decomp} ($3.7\%$ per unit of procurement revenue share) is much smaller than the $14\%$ extensive-margin coefficient because most of the binary effect is productivity selection at the moment of first procurement entry---$76\%$ per the DLW decomposition in the same section---not per-contract rent extraction. Second, the within-firm contract-level pass-through coefficient $\hat{\beta} = 0.81$ in Section~\ref{sec:welfare} (Table~\ref{tab:contract_welfare}) is the empirical bridge from the firm-year markup to the contract-level Rel\_Price ratio, closing the loop between the estimate reported here and the fiscal-welfare calculation of Section~\ref{sec:welfare}.

\subsection{Aggregate Trends}\label{sec:aggregate}

To place the procurement premium in broader context, aggregate markup trends are constructed following De Loecker, Eeckhout, and Unger \cite{DeLoekerEeckhout2020}. For each year $t$, the sales-weighted aggregate markup is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dleu_agg}
    M_t^{sw} = \sum_i \omega_{it} \mu_{it}^A, \quad \omega_{it} = \frac{\text{sales}_{it}}{\sum_j \text{sales}_{jt}}.
\end{equation}
Figure~\ref{fig:dleu_aggregate} plots four specifications. Unlike the secular rise documented by DLEU for US manufacturing (1.21 in 1980 to 1.61 in 2016), Czech construction exhibits a \emph{declining} aggregate markup, from 2.45 in 2007 to 2.08 in 2021. The sales-weighted aggregate consistently exceeds the simple mean by 0.20--0.40 markup points, indicating that larger firms carry systematically higher markups.

\section{The Procurement Markup Premium}\label{sec:causal}

The cross-sectional results document a strong association between procurement and markups but do not establish causality. More productive firms may self-select into government tenders, and government contracts may provide stable demand that enables higher prices.

\subsection{Cross-Section and Panel Fixed Effects}\label{sec:panel_fe}

The primary specification exploits within-firm variation in procurement status across a panel of 1,267 firms:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:panel_fe}
    \ln\mu_{it} = \delta_1 \, pp_{it} + \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \nu_{it},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha_i$ and $\gamma_t$ are firm and year fixed effects. The coefficient $\delta_1$ is identified from the 474 switcher firms that change procurement status during the sample period. The estimate is $\hat{\delta}_1 = 0.131$ (SE = 0.005), corresponding to a 14.0\% premium. This is stable across specifications: pooled OLS with year$\times$NACE fixed effects gives 15.0\%, firm FE alone gives 14.8\%, and adding capital and COGS controls gives 14.1\%.

Robustness to omitted variable bias is assessed using Oster \cite{Oster2019}: the bias-adjusted coefficient under equal selection ($\delta = 1$) and $R_{\max} = \min(1.3\tilde{R}, 1)$ yields an identified set of $[0.116, 0.140]$, with $\delta^* = -5.9$---unobservables would need to work in the \emph{opposite} direction to explain away the premium.

A potential SUTVA concern is that procurement firms crowd out private demand for untreated firms. With a 30\% procurement share in Czech construction and plausible displacement rates from the literature, the implied control-group markup depression is bounded at approximately 1--2 percentage points---meaningful but insufficient to explain the full premium.

\subsection{Entry and Exit Dynamics}\label{sec:entry_exit}

Following the exporter markup analysis of De Loecker and Warzynski \cite{DLW2012}, the procurement premium is decomposed into components explained by productivity selection and residual pricing power.

Firms entering public procurement experience an 11.5\% markup increase (Table \ref{tab:dlw_eval}); firms exiting see an 11.2\% decrease. The near-symmetric reversal is the strongest causal evidence: if the premium were driven by pre-existing firm characteristics, exit effects would be zero. The symmetry implies that procurement \emph{causes} markup changes, not merely selects high-markup firms.

\subsection{Event Study}\label{sec:event_study}

Figure~\ref{fig:event_study} plots the markup trajectory around procurement entry ($\tau = -3$ to $+5$, with $\tau = -1$ as reference). Pre-entry coefficients are flat and insignificant ($\tau = -3$: $-0.010$; $\tau = -2$: $-0.008$), validating the parallel trends assumption. The markup jumps sharply at entry ($\tau = 0$: $+0.134$) and remains persistently elevated through $\tau = +5$ ($\approx 0.09$--$0.11$). The absence of pre-trends combined with persistent post-entry effects is the cleanest causal evidence in the paper. As a heterogeneity-robust check, the Sun and Abraham \cite{SunAbraham2021} interaction-weighted estimator implemented via \texttt{fixest::sunab()} yields an aggregated ATT of $0.074$ (SE $0.010$, $p < 10^{-13}$), lower than the TWFE benchmark---consistent with early-entering cohorts carrying larger premiums that inflate the standard TWFE average (see Appendix~\ref{app:sunab} for the comparative event-study plot). Adding firm-specific linear time trends via \texttt{fixest} varying slopes ($\alpha_i + \delta_i t$) yields a premium of $0.132$ (SE $0.005$), virtually unchanged from the plain FE baseline, confirming that the premium is not driven by differential markup trajectories across firms.

\subsection{Productivity Decomposition}\label{sec:productivity_decomp}

Including translog productivity $\hat{\omega}_{it}$ as a control reduces the premium from 13.5\% to 3.2\% ($t = 4.1$). Productivity thus explains 76\% of the procurement-markup association---closely matching DLW's finding that productivity explains approximately 70\% of the export markup premium. The residual 3.2\% represents pricing power beyond cost advantages: procurement firms charge above what their productivity alone would predict, consistent with less elastic government demand or political favoritism \cite{BaranekTitl2024}.

Interacting the procurement dummy with procurement revenue share yields a coefficient of 0.037 ($t = 4.3$): each additional unit of procurement intensity raises markups by 3.7\%. This dose-response relationship reinforces the causal interpretation.

\subsection{Sub-Industry Heterogeneity}\label{sec:heterogeneity}

Interacting procurement status with NACE 2-digit dummies in the \emph{pooled} cross-section reveals heterogeneous premiums: buildings (NACE 41) 16.9\%, civil engineering (NACE 42) 15.5\%, and specialized construction (NACE 43) 7.8\%. The 2:1 ratio between buildings and specialized construction mirrors concentration differences and suggests the premium partly reflects market structure. The \emph{within-firm} (firm FE) premium is largest in buildings (17.4\%, NACE 41) and smallest in civil engineering (6.4\%, NACE 42)---consistent with EU-level tendering rules constraining entry-stage rent-sharing for large infrastructure contracts more than for localized building projects.

\subsection{Czech Transparency Reforms}\label{sec:policy}

The declining premium---from approximately 30\% in 2006 to approximately 10\% in 2021---coincides with the institutional reforms summarized in Table~\ref{tab:reforms}. Full-panel firm fixed effects estimates show a clear structural break around 2012, with the premium falling from approximately 0.30 (2006--2009) to 0.25 (2010--2015) to 0.10 (2016--2021).

An important caveat is that the decline may partly reflect compositional changes: as more firms enter procurement (the procurement share rose from 28\% to 55\%), the marginal entrants may be less productive and less able to extract rents, mechanically reducing the average premium. The within-firm decomposition partially addresses this by showing that within-firm markups of procurement firms also declined.

\subsection{Favoritism versus Productivity}\label{sec:favoritism}

Baran\'{e}k and Titl \cite{BaranekTitl2024} show that political connections raise Czech procurement prices by 6.2\%, with no corresponding gains in project quality, and that EU co-funding oversight fully mitigates this overpricing. Their identification exploits electoral variation: when a connected politician loses power, the firm's contracts immediately return to normal prices.

\input{../input/tables/favoritism_decomposition.tex}

Table~\ref{tab:favoritism} decomposes the markup premium. Firms with above-median single-bid shares exhibit a larger premium than those facing greater competition (Panel~A). The premium declines from pre-2012 to post-2012, consistent with the single-bidding ban reducing favoritism (Panel~B). A naive calibration against Baran\'{e}k and Titl~\cite{BaranekTitl2024}, who estimate 6\% contract-price overpricing from political connections, places an upper bound of approximately 6 percentage points on the favoritism channel, leaving a residual of 7.8 percentage points attributable to productivity selection and non-political rents (Panel~C). Oster \cite{Oster2019} bounds yield $\delta^* = -7.3$, indicating unobservables would need to work in the opposite direction to explain away the premium (Panel~D). Both policy reforms are associated with significant declines in the procurement premium (Panel~E).

Panel~F provides a direct test using engineering cost estimates from the procurement register, and supersedes the Panel~C naive calibration as the paper's main evidence on this decomposition. Procuring authorities produce ex-ante cost estimates for each tender; the ratio of realized price to this estimate ($\mathrm{Rel\_Price}_{jt}$) measures overpricing at the contract level.\footnote{Engineering estimates explain 91\% of price variation in the full Datlab sample (Baran\'{e}k and Titl~\cite{BaranekTitl2024}, Table~1). I aggregate contract-level Rel\_Price to firm-year means, trimming to $(0.2, 5)$ following their methodology.} On the subsample of 358 procurement firms with engineer-estimate data, the baseline premium is $0.149$ (row~1). Adding Rel\_Price as a control reduces it to only $0.141$ (row~2)---overpricing accounts for approximately $5\%$ of the premium, roughly $0.8$ percentage points out of $14$. The Rel\_Price coefficient itself is small (0.017, SE~0.006): firms that bid higher relative to estimates have marginally higher markups, but the effect is economically negligible. Most strikingly, among procurement firms the within-firm correlation between markups and Rel\_Price is essentially zero ($-0.003$, SE~$0.014$; row~3): high-markup firms do not overbid---they produce cheaply. The markup premium reflects productivity selection, not rent extraction through overpricing, and this direct test empirically validates the reconciliation with Baran\'{e}k and Titl's \cite{BaranekTitl2024} $6\%$ price-premium estimate asserted in the introduction. The tender data lack EU co-funding indicators, precluding a direct test of the oversight channel identified by Baran\'{e}k and Titl~\cite{BaranekTitl2024}.

\subsection{Selection on Observables and Overlap Robustness}\label{sec:unconfound}

Following Imbens and Xu \cite{ImbensXu2025}, I estimate the ATT under the unconfoundedness assumption:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cia}
    W_i \perp Y_i(0) \mid X_i, \qquad 0 < \Pr(W_i = 1 \mid X_i) < 1,
\end{equation}
using the eleven-estimator toolkit of Imbens and Xu \cite{ImbensXu2025} on the full pooled cross-section ($N = 7{,}666$ firm-years, 3{,}443 treated). Covariates: $k_{it}$, $\text{cogs}_{it}$, employment (midpoint), foreign-ownership dummy, market share, and NACE 2-digit dummies. Estimators span difference-in-means, regression adjustment, outcome modeling (OM-Reg, OM-GRF), nearest-neighbor matching, propensity-score matching, inverse probability weighting, covariate balancing propensity score (CBPS), entropy balancing, double machine learning (DML), and AIPW-GRF.

Table~\ref{tab:lalonde} reports baseline results. All eleven estimators yield ATT estimates in the 0.14--0.19 range, with a median near 0.15. Regression-adjusted estimators (OLS, OM-Reg, DML) cluster at 0.14--0.16, while matching- and weighting-based methods (PSM, IPW, CBPS) yield slightly higher values. The convergence across methods with heterogeneous assumptions about the propensity-score and outcome models provides robust evidence that the procurement premium is not an artifact of a particular identification strategy within the CIA paradigm.

\subsubsection{Overlap and Trimming}\label{sec:unconfound_overlap}

A central lesson of Imbens and Xu \cite{ImbensXu2025} and the earlier NBER lecture notes of Imbens and Wooldridge \cite{ImbensWooldridge2007} is that propensity-score overlap drives estimator concordance. Figure~\ref{fig:lalonde_overlap} plots the generalized random forest propensity distribution estimated on the Czech panel, and Figure~\ref{fig:lalonde_histograms} shows the treated and control histograms before and after trimming in the layout that Imbens and Wooldridge \cite{ImbensWooldridge2007} use for the LaLonde CPS sample. Estimated propensities reach 0.998, triggering the AIPW causal-forest warning that some treated firm-years lie in regions with thin control overlap.

\caption{GRF propensity-score overlap, Czech construction panel}\label{fig:lalonde_overlap}

\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../input/analysis_figures/lalonde_ps_histograms.pdf}
\caption{Propensity-score histograms, Imbens--Wooldridge (2007) layout}\label{fig:lalonde_histograms}

Before estimating any treatment effects I report a pre-estimation balance diagnostic. Imbens and Wooldridge \cite{ImbensWooldridge2007} argue at length (p.\ 34) that the normalized mean difference
\[
    \frac{\bar{X}_T - \bar{X}_C}{\sqrt{(s_T^2 + s_C^2)/2}}
\]
is the correct overlap diagnostic, not the $t$-statistic. Their reasoning is that the $t$-statistic mechanically grows with sample size, so a larger $t$-statistic for the difference between covariate means does not indicate a harder identification problem, whereas a larger normalized difference unambiguously does. Their rule of thumb is that $|\text{diff}/\text{sd}| > 0.25$ is substantial imbalance. Table~\ref{tab:lalonde_balance} reports the normalized differences for the Czech panel before and after trimming. In the full sample, capital ($0.623$) and variable-input intensity ($0.637$) exceed the $0.25$ threshold by roughly $2.5$ times, and employment ($0.347$) and the NACE 42 indicator ($0.264$) are also substantial. Trimming reduces the capital and variable-input imbalances to $0.518$ and $0.526$ but does not eliminate them, and the market share imbalance actually rises slightly. This diagnostic is useful for interpreting the estimator spread below: the residual imbalance on $k$ and cogs is exactly the source of the entropy-balancing variance inflation in the full sample.

I apply a symmetric propensity-score trim $\hat{\pi}(X_{it}) \in [0.1, 0.9]$ following Crump, Hotz, Imbens, and Mitnik \cite{CrumpHotz2009}, which is the exact rule Imbens and Wooldridge \cite{ImbensWooldridge2007} also endorse as an approximate optimum under standard propensity distributions. \subsubsection{Sensitivity to Unobserved Confounders}\label{sec:unconfound_sens}

The CIA in~\eqref{eq:cia} is untestable. Cinelli and Hazlett~\cite{CinelliHazlett2020} bound the sensitivity of the linear regression adjustment to an unobserved confounder via the partial-$R^2$ framework implemented in the \texttt{sensemakr} package. Table~\ref{tab:lalonde_sens} reports robustness values and benchmark bounds for the OLS regression adjustment.

An unobserved confounder would need to explain $51.2\%$ of the residual variance of \emph{both} the procurement indicator and the log markup to drive the point estimate to zero, and $50.2\%$ to erase statistical significance at the 5\% level. Benchmark bounds relative to variable-input intensity ($\text{cogs}$), the strongest linear predictor among the observables, confirm this resilience: a confounder three times as strong as $\text{cogs}$ reduces the estimate from 0.1407 to 0.1002, keeping the $t$-statistic above $93$. Confounders benchmarked against employment or market share barely move the estimate at all. The Cinelli--Hazlett robustness value agrees in direction with the Oster \cite{Oster2019} $\delta^\star = -6.05$ bound reported earlier: classical and modern sensitivity diagnostics both indicate the premium is not attributable to selection on unobservables.

\subsubsection{Pre-Treatment Placebo}\label{sec:unconfound_placebo}

The strongest empirical test of CIA is whether future procurement predicts pre-treatment markups: causality cannot run backwards in time. Paralleling the Imbens and Xu \cite{ImbensXu2025} \texttt{re75} placebo, I restrict each firm to its earliest panel year with $pp_{it} = 0$ and use the log markup at that firm-year as a placebo outcome. The placebo treatment indicator is a flag for ``ever later treated'' in the panel. The resulting pre-treatment cross-section contains 1{,}025 firms (483 eventual winners, 542 never winners) after dropping the 273 firms whose first panel observation already has $pp_{it} = 1$.

Table~\ref{tab:lalonde_placebo} reports the eleven-method batch on this placebo dataset. All eleven estimators yield placebo ATTs in the interval $[-0.0049,\ 0.0112]$, with every 95\% confidence interval containing zero. The average placebo ATT across methods is approximately 0.003, compared with an average main-analysis ATT near 0.16---a factor of fifty. Conditional on the observable covariates, there is no differential pre-treatment log markup between firms that will later win procurement contracts and firms that never will.

\subsection{Panel Data Methods}\label{sec:panel}

The unconfoundedness design of Section~\ref{sec:unconfound} addresses selection on observables but requires the strong assumption that all relevant confounders are captured by pre-treatment covariates. Panel data methods can address selection on time-invariant and latent-factor unobservables by exploiting within-firm variation in procurement status. The Czech construction panel has $N = 7{,}666$ firm-years and $1{,}298$ firms, of which 791 are ever treated and 507 are never treated.

\textbf{Staggered DiD estimators.} Because $pp_{it}$ is a year-specific indicator rather than an absorbing state, I report panel treatment effects under both the contemporaneous definition (naive two-way fixed effects) and an absorbing reformulation in which each firm's cohort is the earliest year with $pp_{it} = 1$ and all subsequent firm-years are classified as treated. Under staggered adoption with heterogeneous treatment effects, the naive TWFE estimator is biased (Goodman-Bacon \cite{GoodmanBacon2021}; de Chaisemartin and D'Haultf\oe uille \cite{DeChaisemartinDHaultfoeuille2020}). I therefore apply the three canonical heterogeneity-robust staggered-DiD estimators to the absorbing reformulation: the group-time average treatment effects of Callaway and Sant'Anna \cite{CallawaySantAnna2021}, the interaction-weighted event study of Sun and Abraham \cite{SunAbraham2021}, and the imputation estimator of Borusyak, Jaravel, and Spiess \cite{BorusyakJaravel2024}.

\input{../input/tables/panel_treatment_effects.tex}

Table~\ref{tab:panel_treatment} reports all five estimates. The naive TWFE estimator gives the largest estimate, 0.132 (SE 0.004), consistent with the theoretical prediction that naive TWFE overweights early-treated cohorts under heterogeneous treatment effects. The three heterogeneity-robust estimators yield a tighter cluster: Callaway-Sant'Anna at 0.104 (SE 0.011), Sun-Abraham at 0.091 (SE 0.006), and BJS imputation at 0.124 (SE 0.006). The gap between naive TWFE (0.132) and Sun-Abraham (0.091), roughly four percentage points, is a direct measurement of the staggered-adoption bias that the 2021 papers documented; it is meaningful but does not change the sign or statistical significance of the premium. The Callaway-Sant'Anna dynamic event-time pattern is especially clean: pre-treatment coefficients at $\tau = -3, -2, -1$ are $-0.001$, $+0.002$, and $+0.005$ (all within one standard error of zero), there is a sharp on-impact jump of $+0.143$ at $\tau = 0$, and the post-treatment path stabilizes near $0.09$--$0.10$ through $\tau = +5$.

\textbf{Goodman-Bacon decomposition.} To open the naive TWFE estimator and quantify how much of its weight lands on the ``forbidden'' comparisons that use already-treated firms as implicit controls, I apply the decomposition of Goodman-Bacon \cite{GoodmanBacon2021} via the \texttt{bacondecomp} Stata package. The decomposition requires a balanced panel and is therefore applied to a sub-panel of 45 firms observed in every year, on which the absorbing-treatment TWFE coefficient is 0.050 (SE 0.008). Table~\ref{tab:panel_bacon} expresses this estimate as a weighted average of 2$\times$2 DiD comparisons from four classes. About 63\% of the total weight lands on ``clean'' comparisons that use never-treated firms (16.5\%) or always-treated firms (46.3\%) as controls, while the remaining 37\% lands on timing-vs-timing comparisons in which already-treated firms serve as controls for later-treated firms. Within the forbidden block, the average 2$\times$2 DiD estimates are 0.063 for Earlier-vs-Later and 0.046 for Later-vs-Earlier---of the same sign and similar order of magnitude as the clean comparisons (0.050 and 0.044). The forbidden comparisons therefore contribute about 39\% of the total absorbing-TWFE estimate, but they do not pull the total in a qualitatively different direction. This is consistent with the moderate gap between naive TWFE and Sun-Abraham in Table~\ref{tab:panel_treatment} and suggests that the staggered-adoption bias, while measurable, is mild on the Czech construction panel.

\input{../input/tables/panel_bacon.tex}

\textbf{Counterfactual estimators.} On the same unbalanced panel, I apply three counterfactual estimators from the \texttt{fect} R package of Liu, Wang, and Xu \cite{LiuWangXu2022}, which handle the non-absorbing nature of $pp_{it}$ natively rather than requiring the absorbing reformulation. A key advantage over the staggered-DiD estimators above is that \texttt{fect} respects treatment \emph{reversals}---275 firms in the Czech panel switch procurement status at least twice---by imputing $Y_{it}(0)$ from control and pre-treatment observations via the estimated factor structure. The two-way fixed-effect counterfactual (FEct) yields ATT $= 0.118$ (SE $0.008$); the interactive fixed-effect version with cross-validated rank $r = 1$ (IFEct) gives $0.131$ (SE $0.012$); and matrix completion (MC) gives $0.122$ (SE $0.007$). Panel~B of Table~\ref{tab:fect} reports FEct ATTs by CZ-NACE sub-industry: buildings (NACE~41) exhibit the strongest premium at $0.167$ (SE $0.005$), specialized trades (NACE~43) at $0.106$ (SE $0.007$), and civil engineering (NACE~42) is statistically indistinguishable from zero at $-0.001$ (SE $0.029$)---consistent with the CATT and DML-CATE heterogeneity documented in Sections~\ref{sec:unconfound_catt} and~\ref{sec:welfare}.

\textbf{Sensitivity to parallel-trend violations.} The staggered-DiD estimators rely on a parallel-trends assumption that is untestable by construction. The underlying event study is a two-way fixed-effects regression of $\log \mu^A_{it}$ on relative-year dummies over the window $\tau \in [-5, +5]$ (with $\tau = -1$ as the omitted reference), firm and year fixed effects, and firm-clustered standard errors. The breakdown value $\bar{M}^\star$ at which the robust CI first includes zero is strictly greater than two, meaning the procurement premium is robust to parallel-trend violations more than twice as large as anything observed in the pre-treatment window.

\section{Robustness}\label{sec:robustness}

The specifications that follow should be read as features of a single underlying data-generating process---the ACF translog production function with markup formula $\mu_{it} = \theta^V_{it}/\alpha^V_{it}$---not as competing models. Each robustness layer perturbs one ingredient of this common structure: the variable input definition (\S\ref{sec:nine_methods}), the Markov specification for productivity (\S\ref{sec:cwdl}), the instrument set (\S\ref{sec:adl}, \S\ref{sec:abgrs}), the functional form (Cobb-Douglas vs translog, Section~\ref{sec:pf_coefs}), the identification-robust inference procedure (\S\ref{sec:ags_twostep}), or the estimator entirely (DML partially linear in \S\ref{sec:dml}). The 33 specifications summarized in the specification curve below (Figure~\ref{fig:spec_curve}) therefore represent different estimands of the same structure, not different structures. Where results diverge, the AGS~\cite{AGS2017} load-bearing-moment diagnostic of \S\ref{sec:ags_twostep} pinpoints the moment responsible, and the ABGRS~\cite{ABGRS2025} strong-exclusion check of \S\ref{sec:abgrs} confirms that the divergence does not invalidate the causal summary.

\subsection{CWDL Extensions}\label{sec:cwdl}

Following Collard-Wexler and De Loecker \cite{CWDL2015}, six specifications vary the Markov process, survival correction, and instrument set. The procurement premium is 0.138 (SE = 0.002) under the baseline translog and all variants that include $pp_{it-1}$ in the Markov; it collapses to 0.003 (SE = 0.002) when procurement is excluded from the law of motion. The survival correction has negligible effects (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:cwdl}). The Cobb-Douglas specification (Spec E) yields a premium of 0.138 (SE = 0.002)---effectively identical---confirming that the functional form choice between CD and translog is immaterial for the treatment effect, consistent with the GNR non-identification result.

As an additional robustness check, I implement the CWDL ``FOC-materials'' Leontief specification \cite{CWDLKvsOmega}: materials enter in fixed proportions, with $\beta_m$ calibrated at the median expenditure share ($\hat{\beta}_m = 0.51$). Output is netted of the materials contribution ($\tilde{q}_{it} = \ln(e^{y_{it}} - \hat{\beta}_m e^{m_{it}})$) and the production function is estimated on $(\tilde{q}, k, w)$ using wages as the variable input. The premium under this specification is 0.155 (16.7\%)---close to the COGS baseline of 0.170 (17.0\%). Markup \emph{levels} differ substantially (mean 3.7 vs.\ 2.0) because the labor expenditure share $\alpha^W \approx 0.20$ is much smaller than $\alpha^{\text{cogs}} \approx 0.50$, but the treatment effect is preserved.

\subsection{ADL Imperfect Competition}\label{sec:adl}

Under imperfect competition, input demand depends on the competitive environment, contaminating the scalar unobservable inversion at the heart of proxy variable estimators. Ackerberg and De Loecker \cite{ADL2024} show that this requires conditioning on a sufficient statistic---total competitor output under Cournot, an inclusive value under differentiated products. Demirer \cite{Demirer2025} formalizes this correction as a factor-augmenting-technology extension of ACF, showing that markup estimates are biased when competitive pressure differentially shifts input-augmenting productivity across firms; procurement markets are a natural application because public buyers generate persistent demand shocks that plausibly shift firm-level marginal cost heterogeneously.

This specialization sits within a long tradition in industrial organization. Porter \cite{Porter1983} identified a firm's conduct parameter from observed rotations of the equilibrium locus when exogenous variables shift demand without shifting marginal cost. Bresnahan \cite{Bresnahan1987} formalized the framework as a likelihood-ratio test of Cournot versus Bertrand competition in the 1955 US automobile price war. Berry and Haile \cite{BerryHaile2014} generalized the argument to nonparametric identification in differentiated-products markets with aggregate data, and Ackerberg and De Loecker \cite{ADL2024} specialize it to production-function estimation by conditioning the input-demand inversion on a competitive-environment sufficient statistic. Our control function is the production-function analog of the Berry-Haile nonparametric identification result.

The correction is implemented by defining markets as year $\times$ NACE 2-digit cells, including competitors' total deflated revenue in the first-stage polynomial and Markov transition, and adding oligopoly instruments to the moment conditions. The result is a null: the procurement premium changes by less than 0.005. Production function coefficients shift by 0.01--0.03, but the ratio determining the markup premium is approximately invariant---as expected once the external procurement instrument in the Markov transition (Section~\ref{sec:estimation}) has already absorbed the competitive-environment variation that the ADL correction is designed to capture.

Table~\ref{tab:adl_instruments} reports a systematic instrument comparison under the translog specification across nine instrument-set families. Unlike under Cobb-Douglas---where the premium is invariant at 0.170 because the scalar $\theta$ cancels---under translog with firm-specific output elasticities, the premium is \emph{genuinely} instrument-sensitive. Internal accounting instruments (Rows 1--3) give a premium of 0.114--0.125 with standard errors of 0.91--1.01 on the linear COGS coefficient. Tender-level instruments (Row 4) push the premium below 0.10, reflecting weak first-stage identification from bid counts. Raw year-level external shifters (Row 6) further compress the premium to 0.089--0.094 and inflate the Hansen $J$ to 221--264, because they violate the ABGRS \cite{ABGRS2025} mean-independence requirement (Section~\ref{sec:abgrs}). Residualizing these interactions against the control set restores the premium to 0.124 with a 35\% tighter SE; compressing via Chamberlain \cite{Chamberlain1987} sieve projection yields the tightest estimate at 0.134 (SE = 0.426)---a $2.4\times$ efficiency gain over the standard ACF baseline.

\input{../input/tables/adl_instrument_comparison.tex}

Caveats remain. The market definition is coarse; the Hansen $J$ test rejects across all overidentified specifications ($p < 0.001$), suggesting misspecification at this aggregation level. As a complementary ML-robust check, I also run a DML partially linear IV (Chernozhukov et al.\ \cite{ChernozhukovDML2018}) using the continuous-exposure DiD instrument $Z_{it} = \overline{pp}_i^{\text{pre-2011}} \times \mathbf{1}[t \geq 2012]$, a continuous-intensity difference-in-differences design in the sense of Callaway, Goodman-Bacon, and Sant'Anna \cite{CallawayGoodmanBaconSantAnna2024}, in which firm-level pre-reform procurement exposure $\overline{pp}_i^{\text{pre-2011}}$ interacts with the single-date 2012 single-bidding-ban policy shock. The identifying assumptions are standard DiD parallel-trends and no-anticipation of the reform. Nuisance functions are cross-fitted with Lasso, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting partialling out market share, foreign ownership, and year dummies from both the outcome and the treatment. The resulting DML-IV estimates fall in the range $[0.23, 0.35]$ across the three ML estimators---larger than the OLS baseline $0.13$ and consistent with compliers to the 2012 reform exhibiting above-average markup responses to procurement participation (Table~\ref{tab:dml_iv} in Appendix~\ref{app:dml_iv}). The first-stage partial $R^2$ is $0.01$ once firm fixed effects and year dummies are partialled out, placing the DML-IV in weak-instrument territory: the continuous-exposure interaction is nearly collinear with the year dummies after firm demeaning, and the resulting dispersion across ML estimators ($[0.23, 0.35]$) reflects that weakness. These estimates are informative about the \emph{sign} of complier heterogeneity (compliers have above-average premiums) but should not be interpreted as precise point estimates. The positive sign is the substantive finding; the magnitude is unreliable under weak identification.

\subsection{Strong Exclusion Under Misspecification}\label{sec:abgrs}

Andrews, Barahona, Gentzkow, Rambachan, and Shapiro \cite{ABGRS2025} formalize when structural estimates are reliable under model misspecification. Their central result: estimators are ``approximately causally consistent'' only if they satisfy \emph{strong exclusion}---the moment conditions must rely on instruments that are mean-independent of the included controls. If this fails, misspecifying the control function corrupts the causal estimate even when the instruments are valid in the unconditional sense. Their Section~IV.D extends the framework directly to dynamic production function models with input endogeneity, the exact setting of ACF markup estimation. This diagnostic is the empirical counterpart to the Pakes \cite{Pakes2021AnnRev} concern that revenue-based PF estimation does not recover a structural output elasticity for multiproduct firms (see the discussion in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity}): under strong exclusion, the procurement treatment-effect differential is approximately identified even when this concern binds on markup \emph{levels}.

I implement the ABGRS diagnostic using the Chernozhukov et al.~\cite{ChernozhukovDML2018} DML orthogonal-moment construction, which is functionally equivalent to the flexible residualization recipe of ABGRS \cite{ABGRS2025} Online Appendix~C.3: both partial out candidate instruments against the control set via cross-fitted nonparametric regression and test whether the residualized instruments are mean-independent of the controls. For each ACF instrument $Z \in \{k_{it-1}, \text{cogs}_{it-1}, pp_{it-1}, pp_{it-2}\}$, I compute the partial $R^2$ of $Z$ on the year $\times$ NACE 2-digit control set, $\text{partial\,}R^2 = 1 - \text{Var}(Z - \hat h(C))/\text{Var}(Z)$, where $\hat h$ is estimated by Lasso, Random Forest, and Gradient Boosting in turn. Table~\ref{tab:strong_exclusion} reports the results on $5{,}236$ firm-year observations (the translog overidentification sample after requiring lagged instruments). All ACF instruments have partial $R^2 < 0.10$ across all three ML estimators---lagged capital at $0.03$, lagged COGS at $0.01$--$0.02$, and lagged procurement (at $t{-}1$ and $t{-}2$) at $0.08$--$0.09$---indicating that the instruments are approximately mean-independent of the control set and strong exclusion holds empirically. Under the ABGRS \cite{ABGRS2025} Online Appendix~D.3.4 dynamic-strong-exclusion framework, this is the binding check: standard Blundell-Bond instruments $(1, k_{it}, k_{it-1}, \text{cogs}_{it-1})$ cannot satisfy dynamic strong exclusion because they are fully determined by the state-variable control set $X_{i,j}$, so identification of the premium rests on having $pp_{it-1}$ as a \emph{genuine external shifter} predetermined with respect to period-$t$ input choices---the role played by procurement status in our estimation. As a second check, I re-estimate the headline premium with the full DML orthogonal score using gradient-boosted nuisance functions: the residualized premium is $0.1286$ (SE $0.0041$), within $0.0006$ of the OLS baseline $0.1280$, confirming that control-structure misspecification cannot corrupt the estimate because the instruments do not load on control-variable variation.

\input{../input/tables/strong_exclusion_scores.tex}

The partial $R^2$ diagnostic tests mean independence of the \emph{standard} ACF instruments. To further enforce strong exclusion, I implement the ABGRS direct procedure (\S IV.D): each of seven external exogenous shifters---CZK/EUR exchange rate, industry PPI, construction cost index, 10-year Maastricht bond yield, building permits, frost days, and annual precipitation (sourced from Eurostat, CNB, and Open-Meteo)---is interacted with firm-level $k_{it}$ and $L.\text{cogs}_{it}$, producing 14 candidate instruments. Each interaction is OLS-residualized against the controls $X_i = (k, L.\text{cogs}, pp, \text{year FE}, \text{NACE FE})$, so the resulting moments are mean-independent of $X_i$ by construction. Row 8 of Table~\ref{tab:adl_instruments} reports: the procurement premium is $0.124$ (SE $= 0.595$ on the linear COGS coefficient)---matching the best internal-accounting row exactly, but with a 35\% tighter standard error (0.595 vs.\ 0.912).

Row 9 applies Chamberlain's \cite{Chamberlain1987,Chamberlain2022} optimal-instrument sieve compression to the same residualized pool: the observation-level Jacobian $\partial\xi/\partial\beta$ is projected onto a quadratic sieve basis of the 14 instruments, producing $K_\beta = 6$ efficient combinations $Z^* = \widehat{E}[\partial\xi/\partial\beta \mid Z]$. Chamberlain's (2022) sequential-moment extension justifies this construction in the predetermined-input case that matches our Kim-Luo-Su overidentification timing. This follows the pyblp two-step procedure \cite{ConlonGortmaker2020} and is the supply-side analog of Ackerberg and De Loecker's \cite{ADL2024} (\S4.2) approximation to optimal oligopoly instruments. The result is the paper's tightest translog estimate: premium $= 0.134$ (SE $= 0.426$), a $2.4\times$ efficiency gain over the standard ACF baseline (SE $= 1.007$). The system is just-identified ($J =$ n/a), and the mean output elasticity at the sample midpoint is 1.00---consistent with approximate constant returns in the variable input.

\subsection{Transparency: What Drives the Translog Estimates?}\label{sec:ags_twostep}

Output elasticities in our translog ACF specification depend primarily on the firm-year orthogonality between the productivity innovation $\xi_{it}$ and lagged variable-input usage, $\E[\xi_{it} \cdot L.c_{it}] = 0$. This is the core Markov moment that Ackerberg, Caves, and Frazer \cite{ACF2015} identify through the timing assumption that last period's cost-of-goods-sold is predetermined with respect to this period's productivity shock. Every other moment---lagged capital, the translog cross-products $k^2$, $c^2$, $k \cdot c$, and the Kim-Luo-Su deeper lags $L.k$ and $L^2.c$---serves mainly to tie down the curvature of the production surface at given values of $\hat\beta_c$. To quantify this formally, I follow Andrews, Gentzkow, and Shapiro \cite{AGS2017} (as applied by Kreindler \cite{Kreindler2024} in a related nonlinear-GMM setting) and report the scaled sensitivity matrix
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ags_lambda}
    \hat\Lambda = -\left(\hat G' \hat W \hat G\right)^{-1} \hat G' \hat W \cdot \mathrm{diag}(\hat\sigma),
\end{equation}
where $\hat G$ is the Jacobian of the moment function evaluated at the translog GMM estimate, $\hat W$ is the optimal weighting matrix, and $\hat\sigma$ is the vector of bootstrap standard deviations of the moments. Entry $(k, m)$ of $\hat\Lambda$ is the change in $\hat\beta_k$ induced by a one-standard-deviation violation of moment $m$---a metric scaled to be directly interpretable in units of the parameter.

Table~\ref{tab:ags_twostep_sensitivity} reports $\hat\Lambda$ for the linear translog coefficients $\hat\beta_k$ and $\hat\beta_c$ on NACE 41 (the largest sub-industry). The picture is clean: both parameters load overwhelmingly on the lagged-cogs moment, with $|\hat\Lambda_{\beta_c, L.c}| = 2421$ --- roughly three times the next-largest moment other than the constant. The higher-order translog moments ($k^2$, $L.c^2$, $k \cdot L.c$) have sensitivities two orders of magnitude smaller, confirming that ACF identification in our setting runs through the linear lagged-cogs orthogonality---not through the translog curvature. The deeper Kim-Luo-Su lags ($L.k$, $L^2.c$) contribute modestly, providing the overidentifying tests but not driving the point estimates. The same picture holds for $\hat\beta_c$ on NACE 42 and NACE 43 (not shown).

\input{../input/tables/ags_twostep_sensitivity.tex}

The model propagates first-stage moment uncertainty to parameter estimates in a relatively transparent manner. Suppose a reader worries that the lagged-cogs moment is biased away from zero because the productivity process has a serially correlated component that our first-order Markov specification does not capture---a plausible concern in construction, where learning-by-doing and project-specific know-how generate multi-year persistence. Under a local-misspecification approximation, if this bias were equal to $0.5\hat\sigma_{L.c}$ (i.e., half a bootstrap standard deviation), the linear cogs coefficient would shift by $\hat\Lambda_{\beta_c, L.c} \times 0.5 \approx 1210$ in \emph{unscaled} units, or roughly $0.012$ in the parameter $\hat\beta_c = 1.372$ (NACE 41). Propagating this through the translog markup formula $\mu_{it} = (\hat\beta_c + 2\hat\beta_{c^2} c_{it} + \hat\beta_{kc} k_{it}) / \alpha_{it}$ via the delta method yields a shift in the procurement premium of under $0.5$ percentage points---well within the $2\times$ analytical SE band of $0.005$. The premium is an approximately causally consistent summary in the sense of Andrews et al.\ \cite{ABGRS2025}, Proposition 3: even under local misspecification of the control function, as long as the residualized strong-exclusion moments of Row 8 (Table~\ref{tab:adl_instruments}) hold, the causal summary is preserved.

Beyond pointwise sensitivity, I also report identification-robust confidence sets following Andrews \cite{Andrews2017ECMA}. The Wald CS assumes standard asymptotic normality; the S-statistic CS (Anderson-Rubin style) inverts the GMM criterion $N \cdot g(\beta)' W g(\beta)$ against the $\chi^2_{p,0.95}$ threshold without that assumption. Table~\ref{tab:ags_twostep_ci} shows that the S-statistic CS for the translog coefficients is 2--6 times wider than the Wald CS, indicating moderate identification weakness in the first-stage GMM for the higher-order terms. This does not undermine the second-stage procurement premium---the premium regression is an OLS on generated markups with a $t$-statistic of 32 against firm-clustered SEs---but it does mean that the individual translog coefficients $\hat\beta_{k^2}$, $\hat\beta_{c^2}$, $\hat\beta_{kc}$ should be interpreted with wider uncertainty bounds than their analytical standard errors suggest.

\input{../input/tables/ags_twostep_ci.tex}

Andrews, Chen, and Tecchio \cite{AndrewsChenTecchio2026} show that under local misspecification, the Hansen $J$-statistic bounds the scope for ``weight hacking'': $\sqrt{J}$ measures how many standard errors a researcher can move the GMM point estimate by varying the weighting matrix within the class of valid choices. Under the baseline translog with Kim-Luo-Su overidentification ($df = 2$), the Hansen $J$ is nonzero and the $\sqrt{J}$ bound is informative. Table~\ref{tab:misspec} reports the diagnostic alongside misspecification-robust standard errors for the procurement premium, computed via non-recentered cluster bootstrap (500 replications). The bootstrap SE matches the analytical clustered SE almost exactly, confirming that misspecification does not inflate uncertainty in the \emph{second-stage causal summary} (the procurement premium OLS) even if it matters for the first-stage GMM coefficients individually. This is the quantitative analog of the Andrews \cite{Andrews2025Lecture} distinction between what ``drives'' an estimate (moments load-bearing for parameters) and what an audience cares about (the causal summary).

\subsection{First-Stage and Markov Sensitivity}\label{sec:firststage}

Polynomial order in the first stage (2--5) has negligible effects on the premium: all estimates fall within 0.136--0.140. Enriching the Markov process with higher-order terms in $\omega_{it-1}$ or interactions with NACE dummies adds less than 0.5 percentage points to the Markov $R^2$ and leaves the premium invariant. \subsection{Double/Debiased Machine Learning Robustness}\label{sec:dml}

This subsection re-estimates the procurement markup premium using the Double/Debiased Machine Learning (DML) framework of Chernozhukov et al.~\cite{ChernozhukovDML2018}, which extends the post-double-selection Lasso procedure of Belloni, Chernozhukov, and Hansen \cite{BCH2014JEP} to orthogonal moments and arbitrary ML nuisance estimators. The approach delivers a Neyman-orthogonal score for the partially linear model
\begin{equation}
\log \mu_{it} = \alpha \cdot pp_{it} + g(X_{it}) + \text{firm}_i + \varepsilon_{it},
\label{eq:dml_plr}
\end{equation}
with firm fixed effects absorbed by within-firm demeaning and nuisance functions $g(X) = E[\log \mu \mid X]$ and $m(X) = E[pp \mid X]$ cross-fitted by flexible machine-learning estimators (Lasso, Random Forest, Gradient Boosting). The point estimate is the residual-on-residual regression $\hat\alpha = \sum_i (\log\mu_{it} - \hat g)(pp_{it} - \hat m) / \sum_i (pp_{it} - \hat m)^2$, which is consistent and asymptotically normal under mild requirements on the ML nuisance rates. This provides an independent cross-check of the OLS premium using qualitatively different functional-form assumptions.

\subsubsection{Partially Linear DML for the Premium}\label{sec:dml_plr}

Table~\ref{tab:dml_premium} reports DML estimates under two control sets. Specification~A includes only exogenous market-structure and ownership controls (\texttt{mktshare}, \texttt{foreign}) alongside year fixed effects and within-firm demeaning. All three ML nuisance estimators produce $\hat\alpha \in \{0.132, 0.132, 0.135\}$, essentially identical to the OLS firm-and-year FE baseline of $0.132$ on the same sample. Specification~B adds the ACF-estimated productivity $\hat\omega_{it}$ as a flexible nonparametric confounder; the resulting $\hat\alpha \in \{0.014, 0.018, 0.019\}$ drops to roughly one-tenth of Specification~A, mirroring the paper's earlier finding in Section~\ref{sec:productivity_decomp} that productivity explains 76\% of the procurement-markup association. The DML estimates thus reproduce both the headline and the productivity-decomposed premium without relying on any linear functional-form assumption, confirming that the paper's 14\% headline is not a linear-regression artifact.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity to Unobserved Confounders}\label{sec:dml_sensitivity}

The DML framework admits a Chernozhukov-Cinelli-Newey-Sharma-Syrgkanis \cite{ChernozhukovSensitivity2019} sensitivity analysis that bounds the bias from an unobserved confounder in terms of its partial $R^2$ in both the outcome and treatment equations simultaneously---strictly stronger than the single-parameter Oster~\cite{Oster2019} $\delta^* = -6.05$ already reported in Section~\ref{sec:productivity_decomp}. Table~\ref{tab:dml_sensitivity} and Figure~\ref{fig:dml_sensitivity_contour} report the key diagnostics. The \emph{robustness value} is $\text{RV} = 0.54$: an unobserved confounder would need to explain more than 54\% of the residual variance in \emph{both} $\log \mu$ and $pp_{it}$ (conditional on the observed controls) to flip the sign of the DML estimate. Translated to the Altonji, Elder, and Taber \cite{AltonjiElderTaber2005} equal-selection benchmark, this corresponds to requiring a confounder whose orthogonal explanatory power exceeds roughly half that of the full control set---substantially more demanding than the $r = 3.55$ threshold that Altonji et al.~\cite{AltonjiElderTaber2005} found implausible in the Catholic-schools setting. Because the observed controls (firm fixed effects, NACE, year) are plausibly correlated with any omitted competitive-environment sufficient statistic, the residualized selection ratio in the sense of Diegert, Masten, and Poirier \cite{DiegertMastenPoirier2022} cannot be orthogonalized away; the RV and $\delta^*$ we report are the correct residualized-selection targets, and they discipline only confounders that are orthogonal to the financial control set---political-economy channels and qualification-criterion manipulation require the separate arguments developed by Bara\v{n}ek and Titl \cite{BaranekTitl2024}, which we invoke in Section~\ref{sec:adl}. Benchmarking against observed covariates places this threshold in context: the strongest observed confounder in the outcome equation is $\hat\omega_{it}$ (partial $R^2_Y = 0.79$), whose partial $R^2$ in the treatment equation is $0.46$ and whose implied bias of $0.135$ is enough to push the adjusted estimate to $[-0.003, 0.267]$. In other words, the sensitivity analysis formally confirms the paper's narrative that \emph{productivity} is the one confounder that could nearly eliminate the premium, and no other observed covariate---capital, COGS, market share, foreign ownership---comes close to the RV threshold.

\section{Fiscal Welfare Implications}\label{sec:welfare}

The 14\% procurement markup premium identified in Section~\ref{sec:causal} has a direct fiscal interpretation: every crown the government spends on procurement carries a markup component that reduces the marginal value of public funds \cite{HendrenSprungKeyser2020, FinkelsteinHendren2020}. This section asks whether the firm-level premium materializes as a taxpayer cost on actual contracts, and if so, how concentrated the incidence is. Following Neilson's \cite{NeilsonSEP2025} decomposition of policy effects into a mechanical redistribution channel and a behavioral supply-response channel, I frame the two Kaldor-Hicks benchmarks below as complementary targets: the first describes what a competition-only reform can achieve holding markup levels fixed, the second describes what a structural reduction in market power can achieve. I quantify both using the Datlab register of Czech tenders 2003--2022 ($N = 934{,}722$), of which $197{,}367$ contracts (21.1\% of the universe) record both the engineer estimate (\texttt{lot\_estimated\_price}) and the winning bid price (\texttt{bid\_final\_price}) and survive a Rel.\ Price winsorization at $[0.01, 10]$.

Table~\ref{tab:fiscal_welfare} reports the two benchmarks. Under Benchmark~A, the narrow Kaldor-Hicks counterfactual, capping every procurement contract at its engineer estimate would yield aggregate fiscal savings of $199$~bn~CZK over the sample (3.62\% of subset spending, approximately $10$~bn~CZK per year). Under Benchmark~B, the structural counterfactual, pricing firms at marginal cost using the paper's 14\% markup premium would save $1{,}392$~bn~CZK (12.28\% of total procurement, or $\approx 70$~bn~CZK annually). The $8.66$~percentage-point gap between the two reflects the baseline industry markup already priced into engineer estimates: engineers budget for a ``normal'' construction-industry markup, and only the procurement-specific excess above this baseline is recoverable through competition reforms alone. Structural reductions in market power---a harder policy lever---are required to close the remaining gap.

\input{../input/tables/fiscal_welfare_aggregates.tex}

Figure~\ref{fig:welfare_timeseries} plots the yearly median ratio of winning bids to engineer estimates alongside the annual overspending aggregate. The median ratio falls from $0.97$ in 2006 to a trough of $0.87$ in 2013---coinciding with the 2012 single-bidding ban (Table~\ref{tab:reforms})---and recovers to $0.97$ by 2018 as procedural adjustments erode the reform's competitive impact. Measured in fiscal terms, the 2012--2015 window delivered roughly $1$--$2$ percentage points of additional annual savings (equivalent to $5$--$10$~bn~CZK per year) relative to the pre-reform baseline. This fiscal interpretation complements the paper's DiD estimate of $-0.026$ log points for the 2012 reform, translating a regression coefficient into a concrete taxpayer-surplus magnitude.

Benchmark~B assumes the 14\% firm-level premium passes through uniformly to contract-level prices. I test this assumption by merging the Datlab register to the paper's firm panel via \texttt{bidder\_id}~$\to$~Czech IČO~$\to$~MagnusWeb firm \texttt{id}, yielding $8{,}860$ construction contracts (after dropping exact-duplicate Datlab records) from $408$ firms with both a valid Rel.\ Price and an ACF-estimated markup. Table~\ref{tab:contract_welfare} reports the resulting contract-level regression of $\log(\text{Rel.\ Price}_k)$ on $\log(\text{markup}_{it})$ across three fixed-effects specifications. The cross-firm associations (columns 1--2) are null: differences in average markups across firms do not translate into systematic differences in bid-to-estimate ratios, reflecting sorting of different firm types into different contract portfolios. The \emph{within-firm} relationship (column~3, firm fixed effects) is strongly positive: $\hat\beta = 0.813$ (SE $0.161$, $p < 0.001$), implying that when the same firm's ACF-estimated markup rises by 1\%, its contract-level Rel.\ Price rises by approximately 0.81\%. This near-unit pass-through within firm-years validates the Benchmark~B assumption that the 14\% firm-level premium materializes as excess payments to the government on a contract-by-contract basis---and it localizes the welfare transfer temporally: within-firm markup fluctuations, rather than cross-firm sorting, are the mechanism by which the procurement premium becomes a fiscal cost.

\input{../input/tables/contract_level_welfare.tex}

The incidence of the welfare transfer is highly concentrated. Within the $8{,}860$-contract merged sample, the top $10\%$ of contracts by implied fiscal transfer (ranked on $\text{bid}_k \times (\mu_{it}-1)/\mu_{it}$) account for $72.4\%$ of the total transfer, and the top $1\%$ alone account for $37.7\%$.\footnote{The implied transfer uses the \emph{absolute} ACF markup over marginal cost and therefore includes normal returns to capital; the policy-relevant counterfactual is Benchmark~B of Table~\ref{tab:fiscal_welfare}, which applies only the $14\%$ procurement-specific premium. The concentration statistics reported here are invariant to this distinction because they are computed on the ratio of per-contract contributions to their sum.} The concentration is driven by large-scale civil-engineering projects (NACE~42: roads, motorways, and rail infrastructure) awarded by the Czech road and railway directorates to the three largest Czech contractors---STRABAG, EUROVIA~CS, and Skanska. Appendix~\ref{app:incidence} lists the top twenty contracts by implied transfer, with the single largest (STRABAG's D3 Tábor--Veselí nad Lužnicí motorway, 2017, $9{,}498$~mn~CZK bid) alone representing an implied transfer of $6{,}038$~mn~CZK. This extreme concentration has a direct policy implication: welfare-improving procurement reform can target a small number of very large tenders rather than the full $5$~bn~CZK/year flow of construction contracts.

Table~\ref{tab:contract_welfare_het} decomposes the within-firm pass-through coefficient by NACE sub-industry and by reform era, re-estimating the column-(3) specification of Table~\ref{tab:contract_welfare} on each subset. Panel~A shows that the pass-through is strongly heterogeneous across sub-industries. NACE~42 civil engineering exhibits an \emph{over-full} pass-through of $\hat\beta = 1.340$ (SE $0.184$, $p < 0.001$)---a one-percent increase in a firm's markup translates to a $1.34\%$ increase in the contract-level Rel.\ Price. NACE~41 (buildings) shows a clean unit pass-through at $\hat\beta = 1.010$ (SE $0.243$, $p < 0.001$), consistent with the theoretical benchmark under full rent capture. NACE~43 (specialized trades) is statistically indistinguishable from zero ($\hat\beta = -0.353$, SE $0.336$, $p = 0.29$), consistent with the lower absolute markups observed in this sub-industry (mean $\mu_{\text{ACF}} = 1.66$) and with a more competitive contracting environment for specialized services. Civil engineering, which accounts for $65\%$ of the aggregate implied transfer in the merged sample, is therefore also the sub-industry where the markup-to-price transmission is strongest---a double concentration of the welfare cost in NACE~42 contracts.

The cross-sub-industry level heterogeneity in Panel~A is directly validated by the DML conditional average treatment effects of Table~\ref{tab:dml_cate} in Appendix~\ref{app:dml_cate}, which restrict the Neyman-orthogonal score to each NACE subgroup using the full-sample cross-fitted nuisance functions: NACE~41 buildings $\hat\alpha = 0.160$, NACE~42 civil eng.\ $\hat\alpha = 0.055$, and NACE~43 specialized $\hat\alpha = 0.113$, each within $0.02$ of the \texttt{grf} causal-forest CATT reported in Section~\ref{sec:unconfound_catt}. This pass-through pattern appears to invert the conditional ATT heterogeneity reported in Section~\ref{sec:unconfound_catt}, where NACE~42 has the \emph{smallest} average treatment effect of procurement participation on firm markups ($\widehat{\text{CATT}} = 0.074$, versus $0.181$ for NACE~41 and $0.118$ for NACE~43). The two findings are complementary rather than contradictory: the CATT measures the \emph{extensive-margin} level effect of becoming procurement-active, which the EU-level tendering framework constrains for large civil-engineering contracts; Table~\ref{tab:contract_welfare_het} measures the \emph{intensive-margin} slope at which firm markup fluctuations transmit to contract prices for firms already inside procurement. The two channels combine multiplicatively into the total procurement-to-price effect, roughly $0.074 \times 1.34 \approx 0.099$ for NACE~42 versus $0.181 \times 1.01 \approx 0.183$ for NACE~41---so the total effect still orders buildings ahead of civil engineering, but the mechanism differs: civil-engineering rents are constrained at the entry margin but transmitted nearly point-for-point through prices once inside, while the buildings premium is predominantly an entry-level selection effect.

Panel~B splits the sample around the 2012 single-bidding ban (Act~55/2012, Table~\ref{tab:reforms}). The within-firm pass-through coefficient falls from $\hat\beta_{\text{pre}} = 0.955$ (SE $0.346$, $p < 0.01$) in the $2006$--$2011$ period to $\hat\beta_{\text{post}} = 0.682$ (SE $0.148$, $p < 0.001$) in the $2012$--$2021$ period---a $29\%$ reduction in the slope with which firm markup fluctuations translate to contract prices. This complements the paper's earlier finding in Section~\ref{sec:policy} that the 2012 reform lowered markup \emph{levels} by $2.6$ log points: the reform did not only compress the distribution of firm markups, it also weakened the mechanism by which markup fluctuations become fiscal transfers on individual contracts. The two channels are additive, and their joint effect rationalizes the aggregate time-series decline in the Rel.\ Price ratio documented in Figure~\ref{fig:welfare_timeseries}.

The fiscal welfare calculation complements the BMY decomposition of Section~\ref{sec:aggregate}, which shows that within-firm markups \emph{rose} while the aggregate sales-weighted markup \emph{declined}---a compositional effect driven by reallocation of market share toward lower-markup firms. This reallocation is a separate welfare channel: as De Loecker and Syverson \cite{DLS2021} emphasize, heterogeneous markups induce misallocation of resources toward high-markup (low-quantity) firms, generating efficiency losses distinct from the standard markup triangle. The magnitude of this channel---the gap between actual and counterfactual aggregate markups under uniform market shares---is proportional to the cross-firm variance of markups, which on the Czech panel is 0.26 (NACE~43) to 0.33 (NACE~42). Quantifying the full efficiency loss from markup dispersion requires demand-side data on substitutability that the current analysis lacks, but the direction is clear: the post-reform decline in aggregate markups is driven primarily by reallocation rather than within-firm compression, implying that the reforms changed \emph{who does the work} more than \emph{how much each firm charges}.

Two caveats qualify these estimates. First, engineer estimates are populated for only 23.4\% of the Datlab universe, a subset likely selected toward larger and more formal contracts; the annual averages therefore use subset-weighted ratios rather than naive extrapolation. Second, Benchmark~B applies the construction-specific 14\% premium to the Datlab aggregate, which includes non-construction contracts. The contract-level regression in Table~\ref{tab:contract_welfare} is already restricted to CZ-NACE~F construction, so its within-firm pass-through estimate is directly applicable to the construction subset of Czech procurement; extending this analysis to other sectors requires firm-panel estimates beyond construction and is left to future work.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper establishes three findings on markups and public procurement in Czech construction.

First, the procurement markup premium is approximately 14\%, robust across nine estimation methods, 33 specifications, and multiple identification strategies. The robustness has a clean source: the premium is identified from cross-firm cost-share variation ($\alpha^V_{it}$, observed data), not from the estimated output elasticity ($\theta^V$). This is why specification sensitivity of markup \emph{levels}---which range from 1.02 to 2.17---does not propagate to the treatment effect. The Andrews, Barahona, Gentzkow, Rambachan, and Shapiro \cite{ABGRS2025} strong-exclusion diagnostic verifies that the ACF instruments are mean-independent of the control set (partial $R^2 < 0.10$), so the premium satisfies the ABGRS criterion for approximate causal consistency under local functional-form misspecification. The Cobb-Douglas and translog specifications are treated as equivalently valid throughout, and both yield the same 14\% treatment effect.

Second, the premium declined from 30\% in 2006 to 10\% in 2021, with Act 55/2012 (single-bid ban) and Act 134/2016 (MEAT criteria) associated with DiD estimates of $-2.6$ and $-2.2$ log points respectively. The 2012 reform not only compressed markup \emph{levels} but also weakened the within-firm \emph{pass-through} of markups to contract prices by 29\%, from $\hat\beta = 0.955$ pre-reform to $0.682$ post-reform.

Third, the fiscal welfare cost of the premium is quantifiable and concentrated. Under the narrow Kaldor-Hicks benchmark (capping bids at engineer estimates), Czech procurement overspending totals approximately $10$~bn~CZK per year (3.6\% of procurement spending). Under the structural benchmark (pricing at marginal cost), savings would reach $70$~bn~CZK per year (12.3\%). The top 10\% of contracts account for 72\% of the implied fiscal transfer, concentrated in NACE~42 civil-engineering projects awarded to the three largest contractors. This extreme concentration implies that welfare-improving procurement reform can target a small number of very large tenders rather than the full flow of construction contracts.

The policy implication is direct. The 2012 single-bidding ban delivered a $-2.6$ log-point reduction in markup levels and a $29\%$ reduction in the within-firm markup-to-price pass-through (from $\hat\beta = 0.955$ to $0.682$); the 2016 MEAT criteria shift added an additional $-2.2$ log points. Together they cut the procurement markup premium by roughly two-thirds over fifteen years. For countries with comparable procurement markets and low bidder competition, mandatory competitive tendering is a high-leverage reform---and because $72\%$ of the implied fiscal transfer is concentrated in the top $10\%$ of contracts, welfare gains can be captured by targeting a small number of very large civil-engineering tenders rather than the full flow of construction contracts.

\subsection{Markup Formula Derivation}\label{app:markup_derivation}

The production approach relies on optimal input demand conditions obtained from cost minimization \cite{DLW2012}. Following the derivation of De Loecker and Scott \cite{DeLoeckerScott2025}, I make explicit the implicit perfect-competition-in-input-markets assumption embedded in the canonical markup formula.

\textbf{Cost minimization.} Consider a firm $i$ producing output $Q_{it}$ at time $t$, taking the input prices $P^V_{it}$ and $P^K_{it}$ and productivity $\omega_{it}$ as given. The firm takes dynamic inputs (capital $K_{it}$) as predetermined and chooses the variable input $V_{it}$ to minimize cost subject to an output target. The associated Lagrangian is:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lagrangian}
    \mathcal{L}_{it} = P^V_{it} V_{it} + P^K_{it} K_{it} + \lambda_{it}\bigl(Q_{it} - Q_{it}(V_{it}, K_{it}, \omega_{it})\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_{it}$ is the shadow value of an additional unit of output---equivalently, the marginal cost of producing at $Q_{it}$.

\textbf{First-order condition.} Taking the first-order condition with respect to $V_{it}$, rearranging terms, multiplying both sides by $V_{it}/Q_{it}$, and letting $\mu_{it} \equiv P_{it}/\lambda_{it}$ denote the markup of price over marginal cost:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:foc}
    \frac{\partial Q_{it}(V_{it}, K_{it}, \omega_{it})}{\partial V_{it}} \cdot \frac{V_{it}}{Q_{it}} = \mu_{it} \cdot \frac{P^V_{it} V_{it}}{P_{it} Q_{it}}.
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:foc} holds under any model of output-market competition---it does not require Cournot, Bertrand, or any specific equilibrium, nor does it require a common competition model across firms in the panel. However, because the input price $P^V_{it}$ does not respond to the firm's input decision, equation~\eqref{eq:foc} implicitly assumes \emph{perfect competition in input markets} (or at least exogenous input prices). A growing literature relaxes this assumption to allow for input-market distortions; in construction, the relevant extension is monopsony in local labor markets, which motivates our choice of COGS (materials) rather than wages as the variable input (Section~\ref{sec:variable_input}).

\textbf{Markup formula.} Rearranging equation~\eqref{eq:foc}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:markup_app}
    \mu_{it} = \frac{\theta^V_{it}}{\alpha^V_{it}},
\end{equation}
where $\theta^V_{it} \equiv \partial \ln Q_{it} / \partial \ln V_{it}$ is the output elasticity of the variable input and $\alpha^V_{it} \equiv P^V_{it} V_{it} / (P_{it} Q_{it})$ is the expenditure share of the variable input in revenue. Under perfect competition in both input and output markets, $\mu_{it} = 1$ and the output elasticity equals the expenditure share; deviations indicate market power in at least one market.

\subsection{Technology Types}\label{app:technology_types}

Equation~\eqref{eq:markup_app} is consistent with any of three technology families that differ in how output, the variable input, and materials enter the production function. Following De Loecker and Scott \cite{DeLoeckerScott2025}, I summarize the three cases; each implies a different first-stage regression but the same markup formula.

\textbf{Gross output (GO).} Output enters directly and materials are a separate input:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:go_tech}
    y_{it} = f^{\mathrm{GO}}(v_{it}, k_{it}; \beta) + \omega_{it} + \varepsilon_{it},
\end{equation}
where $f^{\mathrm{GO}}$ takes the Cobb-Douglas or translog functional form of equations~\eqref{eq:cd} and~\eqref{eq:tl}. The variable input for the markup calculation is COGS in our baseline, equivalent to labor in De Loecker and Warzynski \cite{DLW2012}. This is the specification used throughout the paper, with Cobb-Douglas and translog treated as equivalently valid.

\textbf{Value added (VA).} The dependent variable is output net of materials:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:va_tech}
    \ln(Q_{it} - M_{it}) = f^{\mathrm{VA}}(v_{it}, k_{it}; \beta) + \omega_{it} + \varepsilon_{it}.
\end{equation}
Materials drop out of the estimating equation and markups are computed with respect to labor using a value-added-to-output correction: $\theta^L_{it} = \tilde\theta^L_{it} \cdot (Q_{it} - M_{it})/Q_{it}$, where $\tilde\theta^L_{it}$ is the value-added elasticity and $\theta^L_{it}$ is the gross-output elasticity required by equation~\eqref{eq:markup_app}.

\textbf{Leontief / fixed-proportion (LEO).} Output is a fixed-proportion combination of materials and a labor-capital aggregate:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:leo_tech}
    Q_{it} = \min\bigl[\kappa_{it} M_{it},\ F(L_{it}, K_{it}) \exp(\omega_{it})\bigr] \exp(\varepsilon_{it}),
\end{equation}
where $\kappa_{it}$ is the inverse of the per-unit materials requirement. This is Collard-Wexler and De Loecker's \cite{CWDL2015} preferred specification for US steel and De Loecker and Scott's \cite{DeLoeckerScott2025} for US brewing; I report it as a robustness check in Section~\ref{sec:cwdl} with $\hat\beta_M$ calibrated at the median materials expenditure share ($\hat\beta_M = 0.51$). Under Leontief, materials cannot be ignored when computing markups because both materials and labor must increase to raise output. The appropriate markup formula is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:leo_markup}
    \mu_{it} = \frac{\mu^F_{it}}{1 + \alpha^M_{it}\,\mu^F_{it}},
\end{equation}
where $\mu^F_{it} = \theta^L_{it} R_{it} / (P^L_{it} L_{it})$ is the labor-based markup using equation~\eqref{eq:markup_app} and $\alpha^M_{it} = P^M_{it} M_{it}/R_{it}$ is the materials revenue share \cite{DeLoeckerScott2025}. The three technology types are consistent estimators of the same markup under correct specification; Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity} shows that the Czech construction premium is stable across functional forms, providing convergent point-identification evidence under the external-instrument Markov transition of Section~\ref{sec:estimation}, where lagged procurement status $pp_{it-1}$ restores identification despite the Gandhi, Navarro, and Rivers \cite{GNR2020} non-identification result for pure internal-instrument gross-output specifications.

\subsection{ACF Estimation}\label{app:acf}

The ACF estimator proceeds in two stages.

\textbf{Stage 1.} Estimate expected output as a flexible function of observables:
\begin{equation}
    y_{it} = \phi_t(v_{it}, k_{it}, pp_{it}) + \varepsilon_{it},
\end{equation}
where $\phi_t(\cdot)$ is approximated by a third-order polynomial. This yields $\hat{\phi}_{it} = y_{it} - \hat{\varepsilon}_{it}$.

\textbf{Stage 2.} For a candidate parameter vector $\beta$, compute:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\omega}_{it}(\beta) = \hat{\phi}_{it} - f(v_{it}, k_{it}; \beta),
\end{equation}
and estimate the Markov process:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\omega}_{it} = \rho \hat{\omega}_{it-1} + \gamma pp_{it-1} + \delta \hat{p}_{it-1} + \xi_{it},
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p}_{it-1}$ is the predicted survival probability from a probit model. The innovation $\hat{\xi}_{it}(\beta)$ is orthogonal to the instruments $\mathbf{Z}_{it}$:
\begin{equation}
    \E[\hat{\xi}_{it}(\beta) \cdot \mathbf{Z}_{it}] = 0.
\end{equation}
The GMM criterion is minimized over $\beta$ using Nelder-Mead followed by BFGS refinement.

\textbf{Methodological lineage.} The two-step estimator above is a minimum-distance estimator in the sense of Chamberlain \cite{Chamberlain1984} (\S4.2): the orthogonality condition $E[\xi_{it}(\beta) \cdot \mathbf{Z}_{it}] = 0$ represents a restriction on the population expectation $E(w_i)$, and the second-stage criterion function is the quadratic form $[\hat w - g(\beta)]' \hat W [\hat w - g(\beta)]$ with optimal weighting $\hat W = \hat V(w_i)^{-1}$. Chamberlain \cite{Chamberlain2022} extends this to sequential moment restrictions in which the conditioning information set grows over time, the framework that matches our use of $k_{it-1}$ and $\text{cogs}_{it-2}$ as predetermined instruments with feedback from lagged outcomes to current inputs. Ackerberg, Chen, and Hahn \cite{ACH2012} provide the sandwich variance for the two-step plug-in estimator.

\textbf{Standard errors.} I compute analytical standard errors following Ackerberg, Chen, and Hahn \cite{ACH2012}. The GMM sandwich variance is:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{V} = \frac{1}{N} (G'WG)^{-1} G'W \hat{S} W G (G'WG)^{-1},
\end{equation}
where $G$ is the Jacobian of moment conditions, $W$ is the weighting matrix, and $\hat{S}$ is the firm-clustered meat matrix. Standard errors for markups are obtained via the delta method.

\subsection{Translog Output Elasticity}\label{app:translog}

Under the translog specification (Equation \ref{eq:tl}), the output elasticity of the variable input is firm- and time-specific:
\begin{equation}
    \theta^V_{it} = \hat{\beta}_v + 2\hat{\beta}_{vv} v_{it} + \hat{\beta}_{vk} k_{it}.
\end{equation}
The markup is then $\mu_{it} = \theta^V_{it} / \alpha^V_{it}$, where the expenditure share $\alpha^V_{it}$ is computed directly from the data. Under Cobb-Douglas, $\theta^V_{it} = \hat{\beta}_v$ is constant across firms, and markup variation comes entirely from the expenditure share.

\subsection{Moment Conditions}\label{app:moments}

The baseline instrument set for the Cobb-Douglas specification is $\mathbf{Z}_{it} = (1, k_{it}, \text{cogs}_{it-1})$, yielding a just-identified system with 3 moments and 3 parameters ($\beta_0, \beta_k, \beta_{\text{cogs}}$). For the translog, the instrument set expands to include squares and interactions: $\mathbf{Z}_{it} = (1, k_{it}, \text{cogs}_{it-1}, k_{it}^2, \text{cogs}_{it-1}^2, k_{it} \cdot \text{cogs}_{it-1})$, yielding a just-identified system with 6 moments and 6 parameters.

For overidentification tests, I add $k_{it-1}$ and $\text{cogs}_{it-2}$ to the instrument set, providing 2 additional moments. The Hansen $J$ statistic tests the overidentifying restrictions.

\subsection{DGM Validation and Revenue Bias}\label{app:dgm}

I cross-validate all estimates against the De Ridder, Grassi, and Morzenti \cite{DGM2026} Python implementation (ProdFun class), which provides an independent codebase for ACF estimation. The CD estimates match to within 0.001 for the COGS elasticity and 0.002 for the markup mean across all three NACE 2-digit industries. A broader concern is that revenue-based markup estimates may be biased because financial data record revenue, not physical output \cite{Bond2021,KletteGriliches1996}. DGM \cite{DGM2026} address this directly. Under Cobb-Douglas with common technology within sectors, the revenue-to-quantity bias is a sector-level constant that shifts markup \emph{levels} but preserves all cross-firm \emph{variation}, including dispersion and trends. Their simulations confirm a 0.94 correlation between revenue-based and true markups, and French production data validate this at 0.80 in first differences. Since my analysis focuses on the procurement markup \emph{premium}---a cross-firm difference---rather than the average markup level, the revenue proxy does not compromise the treatment effect.

DGM also show that if the variable input faces a wedge---such as adjustment costs or monopsony power---the production approach measures the product of the markup and the input wedge (their Section 2.4.1). This provides an additional justification for using COGS rather than labor as the variable input: labor markets may feature monopsony power \cite{KLMS2025}, which would contaminate labor-based markups with a wage markdown. Materials markets, by contrast, are plausibly competitive in construction, making COGS the cleaner measure of the output elasticity relevant for markup estimation.

\subsection{Raval Overidentification}\label{app:raval}

\subsection{External Instrument Identification}\label{app:gnr}

Gandhi, Navarro, and Rivers \cite{GNR2020} prove that proxy variable methods generically fail to identify the flexible input elasticity for gross-output production functions when only \emph{internal} instruments (lagged inputs) are used. The flexible input appears in both the production function and the control function, creating a collinearity that prevents separate identification of the coefficient.

This non-identification is broken whenever the Markov law of motion for productivity conditions on an \emph{external} shifter that is predetermined with respect to current-period input choices. Following De Loecker \cite{DeLoecker2013} and the detailed discussion in De Loecker and Syverson \cite{DLS2021} (Handbook of Industrial Organization, Vol.~4, Ch.~3, \S5.3), I include lagged procurement status $pp_{it-1}$ in the Markov transition, $\omega_{it} = \rho\,\omega_{it-1} + \gamma\,pp_{it-1} + \delta\,\hat p_{it-1} + \xi_{it}$. Because $pp_{it-1}$ is determined by procurement decisions at $t-1$ and is not a function of the firm's period-$t$ input choices, it provides exogenous variation in the productivity process that identifies $\beta_v$ separately from the collinearity that GNR document. The procurement premium is therefore \emph{point-identified} via the external instrument, and the empirical convergence of premium estimates across Cobb-Douglas, translog, and Blundell-Bond in Section~\ref{sec:sensitivity} is convergent point-identification evidence, not an artifact of non-identification. Ackerberg and De Loecker's \cite{ADLQEC2026} Quality Equivalent Cournot model further supports using revenue (rather than physical quantity) as the dependent variable: within-market revenue variation is at a constant price and thus reflects quality-adjusted quantities, yielding consistent production-function coefficients even when firms' unit definitions differ across construction contracts.

\subsection{Incidence of the Welfare Transfer}\label{app:incidence}

This appendix documents the distribution of the contract-level implied fiscal transfer analyzed in Section~\ref{sec:welfare}. Table~\ref{tab:incidence_top20} lists the top twenty construction contracts in the $8{,}860$-contract merged sample, ranked by $\text{bid}_k \times (\mu_{it}-1)/\mu_{it}$. Seventeen of the top twenty are NACE~42 civil-engineering projects (motorways, rail modernization, metro construction); the three exceptions are large NACE~41/42 tramway and traction-system conversions. The top contract---STRABAG's 2017 D3 Tábor--Veselí nad Lužnicí motorway at $9{,}498$~mn~CZK---is more than 1.6 times larger than the second-ranked contract by bid value and alone accounts for roughly $3\%$ of the total implied transfer across the merged sample.

Three observations emerge from the top-twenty list. First, all twenty winning bids come from just nine firms, with Skanska (DS and parent) and EUROVIA~CS together accounting for ten of the twenty positions---consistent with the high market concentration in Czech large-project construction documented by Palguta and Pertold-Gebicka \cite{PalgutaPertold2017}. Second, the implied per-contract transfer is driven almost entirely by scale rather than by unusually high firm markups: the top-twenty firms have ACF markups in the range $2.27$--$3.17$, close to the sample mean of $2.34$, so the concentration in Table~\ref{tab:incidence_top20} reflects the size distribution of infrastructure tenders rather than exceptional rent capture. Third, the fact that engineer estimates generally track winning bids on these large projects (median Rel.\ Price across the top twenty is $0.97$) indicates that the welfare transfer here is a \emph{structural} markup-over-marginal-cost phenomenon rather than a cost-overrun phenomenon: engineers budget for the competitive-industry markup, and the resulting bids land near those budgets.

\subsection{Sun-Abraham Event Study}\label{app:sunab}

Figure~\ref{fig:sunab_event_study} compares the standard TWFE event study (pink) with the Sun and Abraham \cite{SunAbraham2021} interaction-weighted estimator (blue) on the sample of $5{,}061$ firm-years with a defined procurement entry year (after dropping firms with missing entry year and singleton fixed-effect observations). The two estimators agree closely on pre-trends (both flat and insignificant for $\tau \leq -2$), but the SA post-treatment coefficients are systematically lower: the SA aggregate ATT is $0.074$ (SE $0.010$) versus the full-sample TWFE premium of $\approx 0.13$. The gap reflects heterogeneous treatment timing: early-entering cohorts (firms that began procurement before 2010) carry larger premiums than late-entering cohorts, and the standard TWFE assigns contaminating negative weights to comparisons between early and late cohorts (Goodman-Bacon \cite{GoodmanBacon2021}). The SA estimator corrects this by weighting cohort-specific ATTs by their sample shares. Despite the level difference, the qualitative pattern---flat pre-trends, sharp entry jump, persistent post-entry elevation---is identical across both estimators, confirming that the positive procurement markup effect is not an artifact of TWFE bias.

\subsection{Causal Forest Targeting Diagnostics}\label{app:grf_targeting}

The heterogeneity diagnostics in Section~\ref{sec:unconfound_catt} are strengthened by three additional outputs from the causal forest estimated with firm and year fixed effects absorbed via supplied external estimates $\hat Y_{it}^{\text{FE}}$ and $\hat W_{it}^{\text{FE}}$ (Athey, Tibshirani, and Wager \cite{WagerAthey2018}).

\emph{Best linear projection.} Projecting the forest-estimated CATEs onto NACE dummies and market share yields: $\hat\beta_0 = 0.145$ ($p < 10^{-16}$, the average CATE), $\hat\beta_{\text{NACE 42}} = -0.061$ ($p < 10^{-14}$), $\hat\beta_{\text{NACE 43}} = -0.032$ ($p < 0.001$), and $\hat\beta_{\text{mktshare}} = 0.560$ ($p < 0.001$). The NACE~42 attenuation matches the CATT decomposition of Table~\ref{tab:lalonde_catt}, and the positive market-share coefficient confirms that larger firms benefit more from procurement---consistent with the NACE~42 concentration of the welfare transfer documented in Section~\ref{sec:welfare}.

\emph{QINI curve.} Figure~\ref{fig:grf_qini} reports the Qini curve (Sverdrup et al., via the \texttt{maq} package) for CATE-targeted versus uniform treatment monitoring. Targeting the top $25\%$ of firms by predicted CATE yields roughly $60\%$ of the total heterogeneity gain, with diminishing returns to wider targeting. This mirrors the concentration of the welfare transfer in the top $10\%$ of contracts documented in Appendix~\ref{app:incidence} and provides a formal cost-benefit frontier for a policymaker choosing how many firms to audit.

\emph{Risk versus CATE targeting.} Comparing AUTOC for CATE-based targeting ($\widehat{\text{AUTOC}} = 0.008$, $p = 0.035$) against risk-based targeting (predicting baseline markup levels among untreated firms: $\widehat{\text{AUTOC}} = -0.002$, $p = 0.49$) confirms that the procurement treatment effect heterogeneity is orthogonal to baseline markup levels. Predicting \emph{who has high markups} is uninformative for targeting \emph{who benefits from procurement}---the selection and treatment-effect margins are distinct, reinforcing the paper's emphasis on the extensive-versus-intensive margin decomposition in Section~\ref{sec:welfare}.
